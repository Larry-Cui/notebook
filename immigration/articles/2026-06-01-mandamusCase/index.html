    Mandamus Case Study

    Target Case: Almuhtadi v. Canada (Citizenship and Immigration), 2021 FC 712

    Preliminary Issues

    Style of Cause: Can CBSA be sued as one of the respondents

    The Court agreed that CBSA could be added as respondent:

    [22] The Applicants seek to amend the style of cause to name both the Minister of Citizenship and Immigration (the “Minister”) and the Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness as the Respondents. The Applicants argue this amendment is appropriate because both Ministers are responsible for the admissibility assessment necessary to finalize the Applicants’ permanent residency application. [24] I shall amend the style of cause in accordance with the Applicants’ request. The Minister of Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness plays a key role in determining the Applicants’ admissibility and is thus implicated in the delay at issue.

    Mootness

    The Respondents submitted this application was moot and should therefore be dismissed.

    Justice Gleeson described the doctrine of mootness in Khizar v Canada (Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness), 2021 FC 641:

    [20] The doctrine of mootness holds that the Court may decline to determine a matter where doing so will neither resolve the controversy between the parties nor practically impact the parties’ rights. A controversy must exist not only at the time the proceeding is commenced but also at the time the Court is asked to determine the matter. As such, where events occur after a proceeding is initiated, that resolve or remove the live controversy, the matter will be moot (Borowski v Canada (Attorney General), 1989 CanLII 123 (SCC), [1989] 1 SCR 342 at para 353).

    The Court held that "the controversy between the parties remains unresolved. The documents recently submitted to the Court display that IRCC has begun to process the Applicants’ permanent residency applications, but their applications have yet to be determined. Until a final determination is made, this case is not moot."

    Issues of A Mandamus Case

    As the Court held in para 28, the sole issue for judicial review is whether the issuance of a mandamus order is warranted, and in particular:

    • Is the delay unreasonable?
    • Does the balance of convenience favour the Respondents?

    Analysis

    At para 30 the Court held that mandamus applications must be assessed on the particular facts of each case (Tapie v Canada (Citizenship and Immigration), 2007 FC 1048 at para 7). As confirmed by the Federal Court of Appeal in Apotex v Canada (Attorney General), 1993 CanLII 3004 (FCA), [1994] 1 FC 742, 69 FTR 152 (FCA) at para 55, the following conditions must be met to issue mandamus:

    • There must be a public legal duty to act;
    • The duty must be owed to the applicant;
    • There is a clear right to performance of that duty:
    • Where the duty sought to be enforced is discretionary, consideration must be given to the nature and manner of exercise of that discretion;
    • No other adequate remedy is available to the applicant;
    • The order sought will be of some practical value or effect;
    • There is no equitable bar to the relief sought; and
    • On a “balance of convenience,” an order of mandamus should be issued.

    The issue of whether to grant mandamus also concerns the clear right to the performance of IRCC’s duty to determine the Applicants’ permanent residency application, or more accurately, the reasonableness of the delay during which no such performance has occurred (Abdolkhaleghi v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2005 FC 729 at para 13).

    Is the delay unreasonable?

    As the Court said in para 32, "A delay may be unreasonable if the following three criteria are met (Thomas v Canada (Public Safety and Emergency Preparedness), 2020 FC 164 at para 19, citing Conille v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 1998 CanLII 9097 (FC), [1999] 2 FC 33, 159 FTR 215 (FCTD) (“Conille”) at para 23):"

    • the delay in question is prima facie longer than the nature of the process required;
    • the applicants are not responsible for the delay; and
    • the authority responsible for the delay has not provided satisfactory justification.

    With respect to the first prong, the Court considered the average processing time for the application, and compared it with the actual time of the case (Mersad v Canada (Citizenship and Immigration), 2014 FC 543 at para 17). It held that a regular case would take about 12 month but the application at bar had lasted 57 month so far. Therefore, the Court found "the delay in question is prima facie longer than the nature of the process required."

    At para 37, the Court further held that "there is no uniform length of time for the limit of what is reasonable (Bhatnager v Canada (Minister of Employment & Immigration), 1985 CanLII 5558 (FC), [1985] 2 FC 315, [1985] FCJ No 924 (FC) at para 4). That said, this Court has previously found a delay of two to three years or greater to be unreasonable (Dragan v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2003 FCT 211 (CanLII), 2003 FC 211 at para 55)."

    With respect to the second prong of the Conille test, the evidence would be enough if the applicant's providing of the necessary supporting documentation and paying the required processing fees.

    With respect to the third prong, "whether the Respondents have provided a satisfactory justification for the delay," the Court gave the following reasons in its finding.

    As justification for the delay, the Respondents usually rely upon the blanket statement that security checks are pending and that issuing an order of mandamus would have the effect of aborting an important security investigation. This Court has repeatedly held that such an explanation alone is inadequate (Kanthasamyiyar v Canada (Citizenship and Immigration), 2015 FC 1248 at paras 49-50, citing Abdolkhaleghi v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2005 FC 729 at para 26). The Court required the Respondents to provide details of the security concerns in the material filed for the application.

    The Court further held at para 41 that IRCC’s delay is not justified by the fact that its processing of the Applicants’ permanent residency application is contingent upon outstanding processes at the NSSD, which receives advice from the Canadian Security Intelligence Service (“CSIS”) and the CBSA. The Court said "this principle was affirmed by Justice Harrington in Singh v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2005 FC 544":

    [16] It matters not whether the delays lie within the Minister’s office, or with CSIS. The Minister had a duty to act with reasonable diligence, taking into account that resources may be limited. That duty is not satisfied simply by a delegation to CSIS, which falls within the purview of another Minister.

    The respondents relied upon Seyoboka v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2005 FC 1290 (“Seyoboka”) and Bhatia v Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 2005 FC 1244 (“Bhatia”), to argue that longer delays were reasonable on the basis that there was an outstanding security clearance. But the Court found both cases were distinguishable as they involved new information or depended on other process.

    The application processing covered the "COVID-19 pandemic" period. However, the Court gave the following reason to refute the "COVID-19 pandemic" argument:

    [47] ... As noted by the Applicants, this reasoning is not applicable for the period leading up to March 2020, approximately 3.5 years after the Applicants submitted their application for permanent residency. In the absence of evidence to the contrary, COVID-19 also does not negate the Respondents’ decision-making capacity for the entirety of time subsequent to March 2020. The pandemic was undoubtedly disruptive, but governmental processes have slowly resumed and decisions are being made.

    Does the Balance of Convenience Favour the Respondents?

    This question should be considered from two perspectives. First, from the Respondents' standpoint, the Court must assess the necessity of conducting security screening in order to effectively administer and enforce Canada's immigration laws. Second, from the Applicant's standpoint, the Court must consider the extent to which she and her family have been negatively affected by the prolonged delay.

    In light of its finding that the delay was unreasonable, the Court held that the balance of convenience did not favour the Respondents.

    The Applicant also filed affidavit evidence setting out the specific hardship, uncertainty, and inconvenience that the delay had caused to him and his family.

    The delay caused by IRCC has had a negative affect on my family. Firstly, we continue to live in fear and anxiety. My husband has been especially affected by this. He suffers from depression, for which he takes medication for the past two years. He wakes up with nightmares. We both fear that if our status is not secured in Canada, we will have nowhere to go but Syria, where we fear for our lives. Living in this constant fear and uncertainty has affected our well-being.

    I have not seen my own family in eight years. My mom passed away, but my father is still living in Saudi Arabia. Although I now have a Refugee Travel Document, Saudi Arabia does not accept this document. I desperately want to see my father. He is now thinking of returning to Syria. He is old and it is his home. If he returns to Syria I will never be able to see him again. This would be devastating.

    In light of the above considerations, the Court concluded that the balance of convenience favoured the Applicants.
