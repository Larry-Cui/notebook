# Intentional Torts

## Overview

This Module begins with a brief history of the trespass torts, which are now grouped under the heading “intentional torts.” This history is designed to assist with some of the basic terminology in this area. It leads to the next part, which is an overview of basic elements of intentional torts. We will take note of the **onus of proof, which favours the plaintiff**, compared to negligence claims.

The next parts of the Module set out the rules for the “**trespass to the person**” torts of **assault** and **battery**, including a consideration of **sexual wrongdoing**. We will do a brief overview of **damages** for some intentional torts. We’ll then move on to the tort of false imprisonment and then to two categories of “trespass” actions: **to land** and **to chattels**. That latter topic has also expanded since the olden days of writs. Trespass to chattels now includes two related but distinct tort claims: **conversion**, and **detinue**. Together, those three are known as “interference with chattels.” We’ll finish the Module with a survey of torts related to intentional interference to economic interests and then cover the **defences** available to intentional torts.

## Topic 1: Historical Context

A writ, of course, originated from common law as a court order requiring an action be taken or providing legal authorization for an action. The writs are relevant, but for the most part you don’t need to worry about them; you can just learn the elements of each of the torts we consider in the remainder of the course. A refresher on writs does, however, help students to understand how the remaining torts in the course fit together, how they are distinct from negligence torts we considered in the earlier Modules of this course, and why some of them have unusual rules.

At common law, a plaintiff who had suffered personal injury or property damage could only bring a lawsuit **if their actions fell within certain categories of writs**. 

The first kind of writs were for “directly caused” injuries, and were known as “**trespass writs**”. Claims under these writs could not be for injuries that were the indirect consequence of an action. 

When more and more claims for indirectly caused harm began reaching the courts, the law established writs for indirectly caused injuries, the “**case writs**.” 

At the same time, the law became more concerned with fault, and less with “directness,” so the trespass writs began to incorporate “**intent**” requirements, just as case writs began to require **carelessness**. 

**Trespass Writ**

In the **trespass writs**, the focus was on the defendant’s direct and forceful interference, whether **to the person** (trespass to the person, trespass *vi et armis*, “by force of arms”), **to land** (trespass to land, a.k.a. trespass *quare clausum fregit*) or **to chattels**, a.k.a. goods (trespass *de bonis asportatis*). These torts are commonly called “intentional torts,” but that is not completely accurate, at least when talking about the original versions of the claims. These writs also formed the basis of English criminal laws.

Under these writs, it was the defendant’s direct conduct that was important: a direct, immediate link between the defendant’s act and some form of interference with the plaintiff. The plaintiff only needed to prove this directness. There was no need to prove that the defendant intended the interference with the plaintiff, or that the defendant’s conduct was wrongful in any other way. Nor did the plaintiff need to prove actual damage. Defining “direct” can, however, be difficult.

The policy goal behind these torts, aside from compensation, was to preserve the peace, to, among other things, take away the need for retaliation (i.e. resort to legal action and not violence). **The tort violated the plaintiff’s security and society’s tranquility**, that was the basis of the claim, **not actual physical damage**, though if there was damage, the courts compensated for it.

Today, these categories of writs **no longer exist** in our tort law. **The trespass writs have emerged into our modern day “==intentional torts==,**” a term that includes the three types of trespass claims above, as well as newer claims for intentional interference such as **infliction** of nervous shock and **invasion** of privacy. However, the old rules for trespass writs linger. Many “intentional torts” still require that the plaintiff prove directness and little else: e.g., assault, battery, false imprisonment, interference with chattels, trespass to land. Some intentional torts include both direct and indirect interferences, e.g., intentional infliction of nervous shock, detinue, and conversion.

**Case Writ**

By contrast, the **case writs** required proof of actual loss and involved damage caused indirectly by the defendant’s wrongful conduct. **From the writ on the case grew modern ==negligence law==**, which as you’ll recall is the largest area of tort law, but also **defamation**, **nuisance**, and other **strict liability torts** which we will cover in upcoming Modules.


This potted history of the words “trespass,” “direct,” and “intentional” may feel abstract to you. Don’t worry about making perfect sense of it now. We have looked at it only to lay some groundwork for the readings of the next several weeks. 

## Topic 2: Intentional Torts - Meaning of Intention

At common law, the defendant’s direct conduct was key. Some examples of direct and not direct conduct are:

- Hitting with a club – yes, **direct**: the club is an extension of one’s arm
- Throwing stone – yes, **direct**: no need for constant contact between defendant’s body and the object that strikes the plaintiff
- Placing overdoes of pepper in soup – **not direct**: requires the plaintiff to bring soup to mouth (this example just steps over the direct/indirect line)
- Donating HIV-infected blood – **not direct**: hospital that transfuses blood into plaintiff breaks chain (this is further into indirect territory)

Once the plaintiff has shown that the defendant’s actions were direct, then the **onus shifts to the defendant** to disprove that the defendant acted **intentionally** or **negligently** (i.e., must prove both a lack of intention and a lack of negligence). So you see how that old concept of “directness” is still important in the law.

**Intentional Conduct**

The text begins its discussion of intent at **p 265** with a discussion of **actual intent**, and continues with an explanation of **constructive/imputed intent** and **transferred intent**. 

To understand these concepts of “**intent**” in the context of these torts, you need to realize, first, that intent is not defined so narrowly as to necessarily mean that the defendant intended the very specific outcome in that case: it can be “**imputed**,” and can be “**transferred**.” 

**Intent Defined - Imputed & Transferred Intent**

The text covers intent’s meaning at pp 267-268. 

**To satisfy the “intent” requirement, the defendant must do something on purpose, knowing with substantial certainty what the consequence of the act would be, or desiring that consequence.** It does not matter that the defendant had no intent to injure **the** plaintiff. It is the defendant’s intent to do an act knowing what will result that is important. **This distinction is vital.**

Intent can also be imputed or transferred. 

!!! example ""

    **Imputed intent** (also known as **constructive intent**) is where intent is attributed to the defendant even if the defendant did not intend to cause the damage. Why? Because he knew (or **should have known**) with substantial certainty that the damage would result.

!!! example ""

    **Transferred intent** means that the defendant intended one set of consequences and a different one resulted. The courts will take the intent that the defendant had, and transfer it to the second situation. There are two different ways in which this applies:
    
    Defendant intends to commit tort to one person, but ends up committing tort to another person (different object). By “object” I mean recipient or target: i.e., a different person or thing at the other end of the defendant’s action. The object need not be a **person**: it can be a person’s **property**. The text (at p 267) uses a classic simple example: A intends to hit B, but ends up hitting C instead. You could call this “same tort, different victim.” This principle may apply to a variant called “same victim, different tort.” Defendant intends to commit a certain type of tort, but another type of tort results (different tort). For example, A intends to hit B (battery, but ends up hitting B’s car, trespass to chattel).

**Volition and Intent**

You’ll recall that **volition** is generally an “essential” element for legal liability (see p 43, note 43). The text referenced a case (***Stokes v Carlson*** 240 S.W.2d 132 (Mo. 1951)). The defendant was able to **negate intent** by proving that the act was **involuntary**, or that the defendant lacked capacity at the time of the act. **Voluntariness is required for intent to exist** and the defendant’s act must’ve been consciously directed. However, the onus is on the defendant to prove the absence of voluntariness.

Note that courts sometimes use the terms “voluntary” and “intentional” as synonyms. That is not strictly accurate. **If the act is not voluntary, there is no intentional conduct**, therefore no intentional tort. By contrast, **there can be voluntary conduct which is unintentional**, e.g., due to a lack of capacity as with a child, or someone mentally ill. 

A sleepwalker who trespasses is not liable, nor is someone whose body movements from an **epileptic seizure** cause injury to someone else. In short, it bears repeating that if the defendant’s mind has not directed the movements, the movements are involuntary and there is no tort.

**Note on Children**

Canadian law generally hold that persons who do not have the capacity to understand the consequences of their actions have not acted voluntarily or formed the requisite intent to commit the tort. This usually applies to cases involving **children** of “tender years,” and to the **mentally ill** (as discussed in negligence under mental disability, pp 43-45). 

But there is **no automatic immunity from liability**: it depends on the facts.

Note that where a child does commit a tort in British Columbia, section 3 of the *Parental Liability Act*, SBC 2001, c 45 makes the parents of a child liable for the child’s actions where the child intentionally takes, damages or destroy property of another. Under s 6, the parents’ liability is limited to \$10,000, and s 9 gives the parents the potential defence that they reasonably supervised the child and made reasonable efforts to prevent or discourage the child from the type of activity that was committed.

## Topic 3: Intentional Interference with the Person

This is a large topic encompassing many subtopics and categories. We’ll begin our discussion of **intentional interference with the person** by looking at **assault** and **battery**. 

An assault is generally the **threat** of an imminent battery. Battery is the **actual harmful or offensive** bodily contact. 

As the text notes, it’s possible to have an assault but no battery (i.e. pointing a loaded gun at someone, threaten to shoot, but not shooting). Less common it’s possible to have a battery but no assault (i.e. hitting someone from behind by surprise can be a battery without an assault). **Assault and battery are thus tied together but are independent causes of action**. 

Since an assault in tort law technically precedes a battery we’ll look at assault first (pp 272-75). We’ll then look at other intentional torts that fall under intentional interference with the person.

**Assault**

Assault as a tort is derived from the writ of trespass *vi et armis*, which deals with directly-caused injury to the person, and directly-caused injury is all the plaintiff needs to prove. Civil actions in assault alone are rare. The damages are usually nominal for assault alone (see p 274, note 23). However there are exceptions that deal with repeated and persistent assaults targeting an individual (*ibid*, note 24). **Most assault lawsuits go hand in hand with actions in battery.**

The first thing to remember about assault is that there is **no physical contact** by the defendant to the plaintiff. There is no touching, and no harm need be sustained, this tort is “actionable *per se*.” The elements of assault are twofold:

- **Direct, intentional threat by defendant:** the defendant intentionally and voluntarily makes a threat of immediate harmful or offensive contact
- **Apprehension by plaintiff:** the defendant’s threat created in plaintiff’s mind a subjective but reasonable apprehension of immediate harmful or offensive contact

<u>Subjective but reasonable apprehension vs. actual violence or intent to do violence</u>

The plaintiff must prove that the defendant’s conduct created in the plaintiff’s mind the apprehension of immediate harmful or offensive contact. **It is irrelevant whether the defendant was actually going to carry out the threat. It is whether the plaintiff believed**, with reason, that the threat was going to be carried out and the defendant had the apparent means to carry it out immediately. So in ***Stephens v Myers*** (1830), 4 C. & P. 349, 172 E.R. 735 (N.P.). (p 273, note 19) assault was found, since the plaintiffs perceived the threat of imminent harm by the defendants.

<u>Onus</u>

Once the plaintiff has proven that such apprehension was created, then the **defendant has the burden** of showing that the threat was **not made intentionally or voluntarily**.


**Forms of Assault**

An assault most often involves words accompanied by actions. However, it can be by actions alone (e.g., shaking a fist and running towards someone). Some useful **definitions** of what constitute assault are set out in ***Mainland Sawmills Ltd. v. USW Union Local - 1-3567***, [2007 BCSC 1433 (CanLII)](https://canlii.ca/t/1t1fc){:target=" \_blank"} (see p 274, note 23). The decision is not required reading but may be useful as a reference.

<u>Immediacy and the Effect of Conditions</u>

As noted above in both parts of the definition of assault, **immediacy** is an important **element of proving assault**. If the threat is not of immediate harm, there is no assault. But what if the threat can be carried out immediately, but a condition is attached? The answer is: it depends on the circumstances, but **if there is a condition that takes away a reasonable apprehension of immediate harm, there is no assault**.

One of our case readings, ***Warman v Grosvenor***, [2008 CanLII 57728 (ON SC)](https://canlii.ca/t/21gdw){:target=" \_blank"}, stretches the notion of immediate harm (see p 274, note 24). The Ontario court found that it (anonymous blogs and social-media posts that target a specific person) was an assault, indeed as the text describes a “sustained campaign of terror.” But the decision is fascinating as it notes **specific posted words online that suggest potential harm**. 

**Battery**

Battery is also derived from the trespass writ, involving directly caused injury to the person. A battery is established when there is **direct intentional contact by one to another that is harmful or offensive**. Some points to remember:

- The contact must be **unlawful** in that it was **not consented to** by the plaintiff, however contact that is part of everyday life or accepted social practices (e.g., mere jostling on the bus, or on a narrow bridge) is not battery.
- The contact **does not have to be actual touching** by the defendant to the plaintiff. It can be made **through an object** (e.g., throwing something at another which strikes that person), or **through a substance** (e.g., spitting on someone), nor does the person have to be **aware** of the touching (e.g., they are asleep at the time). It can be contact with objects on the person (e.g., a bag or clothing), or objects associated with the person (e.g., contact with a car that the person is in). This may sound indirect to you, but the law treats it as direct.

As with assault, **no actual damage or injury need be sustained**, as battery is actionable *per se*. **If there is intentional contact that is merely offensive but not harmful, the tort can still be established.** An example of this might be where someone spits at another. No actual physical injury is sustained, but the person’s dignity may be wounded. That person could theoretically sue in battery (although practically speaking it would be unlikely to bring such action). Note that what is “**offensive**” is based on **what a reasonable person would consider offensive**.

The leading case on the tort of battery is ***Bettel et al. v. Yim***, [1978 CanLII 1580 (ON SC)](https://canlii.ca/t/g16gv){:target=" \_blank"} where it was held that **a defendant is liable for all consequences** of a battery, not just those that were foreseeable (see p 270, note 13). 

In ***Bettel***, the court ruled that **foreseeability** of damage (remoteness of damage) component of negligence actions **does not apply to intentional torts**. The defendant intended to shake the boy; therefore intent was seen to encompass all injuries sustained by the plaintiff including the nose injury. You may have the opinion that nose damage is a foreseeable outcome of such an action anyway, but the point is that if you commit a battery you are responsible for all bodily harm that results.

An area of public contention is **disciplining children**, which the text covers under defences (p 308). Are parents or teachers allowed to spank children to discipline them, or would this be considered battery? 

Section 43 of the *Criminal Code*, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46 permits such persons in authority to spank children to discipline them if they use a reasonable amount of force. This section has been challenged as being unconstitutional as affecting the child’s right to security of the person. A 5-member majority of the Supreme Court of Canada ruled in ***Canadian Foundation for Children, Youth and the Law v. Canada (Attorney General)***, [2004 SCC 4 (CanLII)](https://canlii.ca/t/1g990){:target=" \_blank"} that the section was constitutional, as long as it was given a narrower interpretation than some courts had applied in the past. The Court therefore directed that the degree of force applied had to be **minimal**, **causing no lasting injuries** (“minor corrective force of a transitory and trifling nature”), and **applied only to children aged 3 to 12**. 

**Sexual Wrongdoing**

**Sexual wrongdoing is not a separate tort.** For a sexual wrongdoing to be tortious, **it must fit into the definitions of assault or battery** (or others, e.g., invasion of privacy). Victims of sexual wrongdoing, however, are often a special category of claim. Victims bring claims not only to get compensation, but also for **other purpose**: justice, deterrence, punishment, appeasement, empowerment. This affects how law treats such victims and influence some of the exceptions noted below.

<u>A note on sexual wrongdoing</u>

The text refers to “sexual battery” at several points (see p 271, note 14 and p 304, note 145) in terms of making the tort of battery fit more into the nature of sexual wrongdoing. There is also increasing use of the tort of “intentional infliction of nervous shock” to cover sexual harassment cases (see p 283, note 49). 

For our purposes now however it is useful to note the following **two points** relating to assault and battery and sexual wrongdoing:

- In British Columbia, actions involving sexual wrongdoing are **not subject to a limitation period**, whereas those involving assault and battery of a non-sexual nature fall under the **2-year limitation period**. These rules have been in place for a while. In the new BC *Limitation Act*, RSBC 2012, c 12, they are in s 3(1)(i)-(k).
- Conduct that gives rise to civil actions in assault, battery and sexual wrongdoing can also lead to **criminal prosecutions** for assault. Sections 264.1 and 265 of the *Criminal Code* deal with assault, which includes uttering threats, and applying direct or indirect force on another, whether with a weapon or not.

**False imprisonment**

Like the intentional torts of assault and battery, false imprisonment is an interference with the person that is derived from the trespass writ *vi et armis*. It involves **restraining a person against that person’s will**, e.g., by **kidnapping**, **locking in a room**, or even **intimidating**. The plaintiff must prove that the defendant completely restrained the plaintiff, whether physically or psychologically (i.e., the defendant knew or ought to have known with substantial certainty that restraint would result in an attempt to leave).

The restraint may be of short duration, and it is not necessary that the plaintiff be aware of the restraint (e.g., if the plaintiff is drugged or asleep). Many of these cases involve allegations against **police authorities**, **store owners**, or **security guards** who detain people for investigation or questioning.

***Bird v Jones (1845)***, 7 Q.B.R. 742, 115 E.R. 668 (Q.B.) confirms that “**total restraint**” is required to establish the tort (p 275, note 27). The plaintiff's action for false imprisonment failed, since he was not restricted from moving in other directions, or even across the bridge. 

False imprisonment is sometimes accompanied by the use of **physical force**, however it **need not be**. Restraint can also be by **physical barriers** (e.g., a locked door), or through **psychological means such as threats or duress** (e.g. person does not leave, due to a fear of being arrested).

**Malicious prosecution**

The tort of malicious prosecution involves indirectly caused interference. These actions stem from the wrongful criminal prosecutions of an accused. In ***Nelles v. Ontario***, [1989 CanLII 77 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1ft2z){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court did describe the four elements required for the tort of malicious prosecution:

- **Criminal proceedings** must be initiated by the defendant
- Proceedings must terminate **in plaintiff’s favour**
- Proceedings must have been instituted **without reasonable cause**
- Defendant must have been **malicious** 
 
You can probably see that the last **two elements are difficult to prove**. In practice, they bar many of these claims.

Malicious prosecution was revisited by the Supreme Court of Canada recently in another awful case, ***Miazga v. Kvello Estate***, [2009 SCC 51](https://canlii.ca/t/26g27){:target=" \_blank"}. The basis of the Supreme Court’s decision was twofold: 

- (a) **absence of malice** on the part of the prosecutor; and 
- (b) there was **reasonable cause** to commence the proceeding.

**Abuse of process**

Related to, but different from, malicious prosecution is the tort of abuse of process (p 281). In abuse of process claims, the plaintiff must prove the following **four conditions**:

- The defendant initiated **a civil action** (i.e. court process )
- The civil action is against the plaintiff for some **improper or ulterior purpose** unrelated to the subject matter of the claim itself.
- There must also be evidence of some definite act or threat, **extraneous** to the court action itself, that nevertheless **furthers its purpose**.
- Finally, the plaintiff has to prove that he **suffered damages**.

A few key points here. The initiation of litigation itself cannot in itself be an abuse of process. There has to be some “overt act” independent of the litigation that is meant to advance a defendant’s “wrongful purpose” (p 281). Also, Osborne mentions the policy and legal debate over retaining the requirement of an overt act to prove abuse of process. In general, it does seem preferable to retaining that requirement to avoid a “**chilling effect**” (as he describes it) on plaintiffs.

**Intentional infliction of nervous shock**

The common law **barred recovery for intentional acts** of a defendant that merely resulted in **mental suffering without physical harm**. For example, where a defendant makes a threat of some harm in the future to the plaintiff, and the plaintiff suffers emotional distress, the plaintiff is generally unable to sue in trespass for directly caused injury. An action in assault would fail (for **lack of immediacy**), and the plaintiff would have no recourse. To address this problem, courts created the tort of **intentional infliction of nervous shock**.

Recall that a duty of care in negligence may arise to persons who suffer from nervous shock if there is sufficient proximity (relational, locational, temporal). In contrast, in intentional infliction of nervous shock cases, the **elements** that need to be proven are outlined in the following case.

***Wilkinson v Downton*** [1897] 2 Q.B. 57 was the first case to recognize the tort of **intentional infliction of nervous shock** (at p 282, note 47). The court found that to establish the tort, the plaintiff needed to **prove three elements**: 

1. Extreme, flagrant or outrageous **conduct** 
2. **Intent** to cause the plaintiff harm. The defendant does not need to intend the actual harm suffered, but must intend to cause some harm (the intent will be imputed to the actual loss sustained) 
3. Plaintiff suffers **actual psychological harm**. This must be a physical manifestation of illness or psychiatric disorder.

## Topic 3.1: Invasion of Privacy

**Invasion of Privacy**

Invasion of privacy is an area where the courts have grappled with the creation of a new intentional tort. As the text mentions (at p 284) some provinces, including British Columbia, have **privacy acts** legislating a privacy tort. In the rest of the Canada invasion of privacy is dealt with through **common law**.

**A note on privacy in constitutional law**

The law in Canada is clear that there is a fundamental right to privacy, at least, in the sense of direct interference by the state. The right to privacy is discussed in the text under the general common law principles (p 284). It has been described by the Supreme Court of Canada in ***Hunter et al. v. Southam Inc.***, [1984 CanLII 33 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1mgc1){:target=" \_blank"} as “the right to be let alone by other people” and a “right to be secure against encroachment upon the citizens’ reasonable expectation of privacy in a free and democratic society.” (p 287 note 73). 

This right, however, is not a general right of privacy that citizens can use against all intrusions, but a principle underlying specific *Charter* rights. These include **sections 7** (right to life, liberty and security of the person) and **8** (right against unreasonable search and seizure). Some protection is also afforded to individuals under the *Criminal Code*, such as the provisions on wire-tapping, those protections are not at the level of constitutional rights, but the *Charter* can back them up.

**Privacy as a tort: common law**

The question of whether there is a common law tort for invasion of the right to privacy is **unsettled**. 

In the **United States**, there is such a tort. Privacy interests there cover such areas as (1) intrusion on a person’s seclusion (including physical harassment), (2) public disclosure of private facts, (3) negative publications, and (4) misappropriation of a person’s likeness for another’s benefit.

In **Canada**, while some lower court decisions have recognized a cause of action for invasion of privacy and allowed recovery for damages, **the creation of a new tort has not as yet been confirmed by the Supreme Court of Canada**. Rather, many courts have attempted to frame recovery for such actions **under existing common law tort categories such as ==nuisance, trespass or harassment**==.

In 1991, Ontario came close to recognizing a separate tort for invasion of privacy. In ***Roth v. Roth (Gen. Div.)***, [1991 CanLII 7220 (ON SC)](https://canlii.ca/t/g19mz){:target=" \_blank"} referenced in our text on this topic (p 287, note 66), the trial court found that the defendants’ actions in blocking the road to the plaintiff’s cottage and interfering with the plaintiffs’ use of their property constituted “a harassment of the plaintiffs in the enjoyment of their property which is of a kind that a person of normal sensitivity would regard as offensive and intolerable and is an invasion of the plaintiff’s rights of privacy.” 

The court in ***Roth*** referred to other nuisance cases which protect similar interests, but noted their limitations, since such claims are usually reserved to plaintiffs with a proprietary interest in the land on which they are being harassed, guests cannot sue. It supported the creation of a tort of **invasion of privacy**, or **of harassment**, or **both** (since in the United States the two overlap).

In the intervening years Ontario courts have begun to move more confidently towards establishing a privacy tort. This movement has culminated in ***Jones v. Tsige***, [2012 ONCA 32](https://canlii.ca/t/fpnld){:target=" \_blank"}. This decision has, in Ontario at least, firmly established a new tort of “**intrusion upon seclusion**”. There, the defendant’s unlawful access to the plaintiff’s banking and other personal information was actionable under this tort.

The full text of the ***Jones*** decision sets out an almost exhaustive review of the case law in Canada, the U.S., and the U.K. including the following **elements to establish an invasion of privacy**:

- Defendant’s conduct must be **intentional** (including **reckless disregard** to the potential invasion);
- Defendant’s conduct has **invaded** the plaintiff’s private affairs, without justification; and
- **A reasonable person would regard** the invasion as offensive enough to cause harms such as distress, humiliation, or anguish. Here, **proof of actual harm is not required**, be it financial loss or diagnosed mental or physical consequences. The invasion need only meet the criterion of a “reasonable person” regarding the act as offensive enough to suffer such harms.

The ***Jones*** decision establishes the law in Ontario, not across Canada. The **B.C. courts** have not been as liberal. For example, ***Lord and Lord v. A.G. Canada***, [2001 BCSC 212](https://canlii.ca/t/1flzg){:target=" \_blank"} **rejected** the general proposition that **a right to privacy exists in common law** (referenced in our text at p 291, note 93). Given that in BC there is a statute to protect privacy interests, this is unlikely to change.

**Privacy as a tort: Legislation**

British Columbia has joined several provinces in passing legislation to create the tort of invasion of privacy. The *Privacy Act*, [RSBC 1996, c 373](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/00_96373_01){:target=" \_blank"}, s 1 states:

> 1(1) It is a tort, actionable without proof of damage [i.e., per se], for a person, wilfully and without a claim of right, to violate the privacy of another.

The Act does not define what constitutes a “violation of privacy”, this depends on the circumstances of each case, including the nature of the violation and the relationship between the parties involved. 

What about **celebrities** or **public figures** that are the subject of “tell-all” books or unauthorized biographies? Does this fall under invasion of privacy? There may be potential violation of privacy (but not misappropriation of personality, since the latter is more about a proprietary interest – we’ll discuss that further below under intentional interference with economic interests). Also note that the B.C. *Privacy Act*, s 2(3)(a) states that 

> a publication of a private matter is not invasion if the matter is of public interest or is fair comment on a matter of public interest.

**Stalking**

Stalking is another type of conduct where **celebrities** or **public figures** feature prominently as victims, although **ordinary individuals** are often victims as well. Stalking in Canada is generally dealt with as a criminal matter (**criminal harassment**) through s 264(1) of the *Criminal Code* (see p 293, note 105). As the text notes **there is no independent common law tort of stalking**, although examples are given of stalking-like conduct that **amounted to an invasion of privacy** (p 294, note 106).

Provincial legislation may also circumscribe stalking. The Manitoba legislation cited in our text (p 294, note 108) creates a distinct tort of “stalking.” Unlike Manitoba, B.C. does not have that type of broad anti-stalking legislation but deals with stalking (by family members) through its family law legislation. BC Section 1(d)(iii) of the BC *Family Law Act*, [SBC 2011, c 25](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/11025_00){:target=" \_blank"}, defines “family violence” to include “stalking or following of the family member” while Part 9 of the Act offers several court ordered protection methods against stalking (along with other types of family violence).

**Discrimination: Human rights matter**

Another area in which courts have considered creating a new tort is **discrimination**. The leading case is ***Seneca College v. Bhadauria***, [1981 CanLII 29 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1mjln){:target=" \_blank"} which is referenced in our text (p 202, note 102). The plaintiff sued in tort for discrimination by the defendant college. On appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada the court unanimously held that such actions should and could be effectively dealt with under **provincial human rights legislation**, and not through the creation of a new tort at common law.

**Remedies**

Damage awards (or remedies) for assault, battery and sexual wrongdoing are calculated as in personal injury actions in negligence. Damages may be for pecuniary loss and non-pecuniary loss for any harm sustained. 

Damages for assault alone tend to be small. For battery, harm to a person’s dignity can often attract higher damage awards than physical injury, as a person’s dignitary interest is what the tort of battery is meant to protect. 

**Aggravated** damages and **punitive** damages may also be awarded in certain situations. Aggravated damages are meant to provide extra compensation essentially for aggravating factors that make the defendant’s harms worse (i.e. humiliation, degradation, extreme violence accompanying sexual assault). Punitive damages are meant to be strong deterrents to conduct that is viewed as especially intolerable, outrageous or extremely socially unacceptable (i.e. sexual batteries, road rage involving violence). 

The text reviews damage awards under separate remedies for trespass to person, land and chattels.

A good example of the concept of punitive damages is found in ***Whiten v. Pilot Insurance Co.***, [2002 SCC 18](https://canlii.ca/t/51vn){:target=" \_blank"}. It is referenced in our text (p 313, note 177) and is **a leading case** of the Supreme Court of Canada on the applicability of **punitive damages**.

Often, the wrongdoer in these types of actions is “judgement-proof” (i.e., poor) and cannot pay any award of damages. However, this doesn’t always leave the victim with no compensation. For torts that are also crimes, there may be compensation available under provincial legislation to financially assist victims of crime.

In British Columbia, the *Crime Victim Assistance Act*, [SBC 2001, c 38](https://www.bclaws.gov.bc.ca/civix/document/id/complete/statreg/01038_01){:target=" \_blank"} allows for benefits to be paid to victims of crime, their family members, and even those who witness crimes, for pecuniary losses (medical expenses, income loss, etc.). This does not, however, include an amount for non-pecuniary damages.
