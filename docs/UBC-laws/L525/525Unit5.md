#  Extensions of Criminal Liability

## 5.1 Introduction

Criminal law is not solely concerned with a single offender who has completed a criminal offence. The principles of extending criminal liability recognize:

- different **forms of participation** in criminal offences, going beyond the direct perpetrator (or “principal offender”), and
- liability for **incomplete offences or “attempts.”**

??? warning "ss 21, 22 of the *Criminal Code*"

    **Parties to offence**

    **21** 
    
    (1) Every one is a party to an offence who

    (a) actually commits it;

    (b) does or omits to do anything for the purpose of aiding any person to commit it; or

    (c) abets any person in committing it.

    **Common intention**

    (2) Where two or more persons form an intention in common to carry out an unlawful purpose and to assist each other therein and any one of them, in carrying out the common purpose, commits an offence, each of them who knew or ought to have known that the commission of the offence would be a probable consequence of carrying out the common purpose is a party to that offence.

    **Person counselling offence**

    **22** 
    
    (1) Where a person counsels another person to be a party to an offence and that other person is afterwards a party to that offence, the person who counselled is a party to that offence, notwithstanding that the offence was committed in a way different from that which was counselled.

    ***Idem***

    (2) Every one who counsels another person to be a party to an offence is a party to every offence that the other commits in consequence of the counselling that the person who counselled knew or ought to have known was likely to be committed in consequence of the counselling.

    **Definition of counsel**

    (3) For the purposes of this Act, counsel includes procure, solicit or incite.

## 5.2 Principal Offenders

Principal offenders are described in section 21(1)(a) of the *Criminal Code* as the person who “actually commits” the offence. We will examine the legal significance of section 21(1) in this topic, which identifies not only the principal offender, but also those who are a “party” to the offence by “**aiding** or **abetting**” the principal offender.

**Principal Offenders and Parties to Offences**

The decision in ***R. v. Thatcher***, [1987 CanLII 53 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1ftkz){:target=" \_blank"} shows the legal significance of section 21(1) of the *Criminal Code*, which makes the distinction between principal offenders versus aiders/abetters to an offence “legally irrelevant”, they are all **“parties” to the offence, and thus “equally culpable”**.

Should a person who hires a killer be equally culpable (considered just as guilty) as a person who does the killing themselves?

## 5.3 Aiding and Abetting

The most straightforward mode of liability extending beyond principal offenders and co-principals is **aiding** and **abetting**. 

“Aiding” refers to some form of **“material assistance”** (e.g. knowingly serving as a look-out or get-away driver during a robbery), whereas “abetting” refers to **encouragement** (e.g. verbal encouragement) to the person who “actually commits” the offence (i.e. the principal offender).

**Parties to Offences**: s 21 of the *Criminal Code*. See above.

**Actus Reus of Aiding and Abetting**

In ***R. v. Greyeyes***, [1997 CanLII 313 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1fqzd){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada grappled with whether someone who assists the purchaser of illegal drugs is a party to drug trafficking. This case is an important introduction to the **limits of aiding and abetting** as a mode of liability.

***R. v. Greyeyes*** introduced **a new legal argument** in drug cases called “**Agent** for the Purchaser”. Courts now apply the test set out in ***R. v. Greyeyes*** when dealing with such scenarios: did the Accused provided **more that incidental assistance** in facilitating the drug transaction? If so, then s/he can be found guilty under s.21(1) of aiding the offence of drug trafficking.

***Dunlop and Sylvester v. The Queen***, [1979 CanLII 20 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1mkv8){:target=" \_blank"} is the first in a series of decisions that we will read that considesr how the “**mere presence**” of the Accused at a crime scene should be considered in determining whether they are a party to a criminal offence. 

In ***Dunlop and Sylvester***, Justice Dickson (as he then was) for the majority held that:

> A person is **not guilty** merely because he is present at the scene of a crime and does nothing to prevent it. 

However, he added that:

> Presence at the commission of an offence **can be evidence of aiding and abetting, if accompanied by other factors**, such as prior knowledge of the principal offender’s intention to commit the offence or attendance for the purpose of encouragement.

It is important to note that this **includes an element of mental fault**: “A person cannot properly be convicted of aiding/abetting in the commission of acts which he does not know may or are intended.”

In ***R. v. Jackson***, [2007 SCC 52](https://canlii.ca/t/1v0j4){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada returns to the issue of what role the presence of an Accused should have on the aiding/abetting analysis. As you read this case, apply the concepts set out in ***Dunlop and Sylvester***, was Jackson merely present, or were there other factors at play?

These brief excerpts from two supplementary cases identify the following additional principles/scenarios related to **presence of the Accused** and **aiding/abetting**:

***R. v. Nixon***, [1990 CanLII 10993 (BC CA)](https://canlii.ca/t/g9cfp){:target=" \_blank"}: In upholding the conviction, Justice Legg stated

> An accused who is present at the scene of an offence and who carries out no overt acts to aid or encourage the commission of the offence may none the less be convicted as a party if **his purpose in failing to act was to aid in the commission of the offence**. 

Factors relevant in this case included:

- Presence of the Accused at the time of the assault
- Knowledge of the assault
- Statutory duty to protect the victim
- **Failure to act amounts to encouragement** of the principal offender

***R. v. Popen***, [1981 CanLII 3345 (ON CA)](https://canlii.ca/t/gb3r9){:target=" \_blank"}:

> In some circumstances, a person who is present at the commission of an illegal act, which he has a duty to prevent, may **by mere inactivity encourage the illegal act**.

***Mens Rea* of Aiding and Abetting**

Recall that Section 21(1) of the *Criminal Code* encompasses anyone who “does or omits to do anything **for the purpose of** aiding any person to commit [an offence]” (emphasis added). The following decisions consider what level of mental fault this requires.

***R. v. Briscoe***, [2010 SCC 13](https://canlii.ca/t/29280){:target=" \_blank"}

As you read through this decision, focus on paragraphs **13 to 17** and the court’s discussion of how *mens rea* is analysed in the context of an aiding and abetting case.

> [16] The *mens rea* requirement reflected in the word “purpose” under s. 21(1)(b) has two components: **intent** and **knowledge**. For the intent component, it was settled in ***R. v. Hibbert***, [1995] 2 S.C.R. 973, that “**purpose**” in s. 21(1)(b) should be understood as essentially **synonymous with “intention”**. The Crown must prove that the accused intended to assist the principal in the commission of the offence. The Court emphasized that “purpose” should not be interpreted as incorporating the notion of “desire” into the fault requirement for party liability. It is therefore **not required that the accused desired that the offence be successfully committed** (***Hibbert***, at para. 35).

> [17] As for **knowledge**, in order to have the intention to assist in the commission of an offence, the aider must know that the perpetrator intends to commit the crime, although **he or she need not know precisely how it will be committed**. That sufficient knowledge is a prerequisite for intention is simply a matter of common sense.

## 5.4 Common Intention

Common intention is the most complicated mode of liability that we will learn, but it is also the most interesting and potentially controversial. It expands the net of criminal responsibility beyond the principal offender, co-principals (if any), aiders/abetters (if any), to **persons who do not actually assist or encourage the commission of the offence** for which they are charged as a party.

**Common Intention**: **s.21(2)** of the *Criminal Code*.

Here is the section broken down into its base components. Note the important role *mens rea* plays in s.21(2).

!!! warning "21(2) Where two or more persons"

    - form an **intention in common**
    - to carry out an **unlawful purpose** and to **assist each other** therein
    - and any one of them, in carrying out the common purpose, **commits an offence**
    - each of them who **knew or ought to have known** that the commission of the **offence** would be a probable consequence of carrying out the common purpose
    - is a party to that offence.

The Supreme Court of Canada decision in ***R. v. Kirkness***, [1990 CanLII 57 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1fsqc){:target=" \_blank"} recognizes a number of **key principles with respect to common intention**. 

Note the following points of law from **Justice Wilson**'s dissenting opinion:

- common intention **need not be pre-planned** in any way. it is sufficient that it arise[s] just prior to or at the time of the commission of the offence.
- The essential elements of common intention are usually "**implied from the facts**"
- Where one has **aided or abetted** in the commission of an offence, there can be little doubt that **a shared intention** to effect an unlawful purpose existed
- To abandon participation in the unlawful purpose, "**abandonment**" (which can be a defence for common intention) requires "something more than a mere mental change of intention and physical change of place; where practicable and reasonable there must be timely communication of the intention to abandon the common purpose to those who desire to continue it; defendants will be held to a different standard depending upon the degree of participation in the crime
- A causal, but not a temporal **connection, is required** between the unlawful act and the ultimate offence

**Constitutional Issues with Common Intention**

The potential for someone to be liable through common intention for an offence that they "ought to have known" would be a probable consequence of carrying out the common purpose (*Criminal Code*, section 21(2)) raises potential constitutional issues due to the **objective fault** level that it creates.

Based on what we have already learned about when objective mental fault is constitutionally impermissible, do you think section 21(2) is valid as worded for all criminal offences? If not, which offences are excluded from its ambit? After you have considered these questions, read the Supreme Court of Canada decision below to see if your answers were correct.

In ***R. v. Logan***, [1990 CanLII 84 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1fssn){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada considered the constitutional validity of the "**ought to have known**" aspect of common intention. 

It found that it is a constitutional requirement under s.7 of the *Charter* for some offences to have subjective *mens rea*. This is due to the seriousness of those offences and the stigma involved in their conviction.

However, **for other offences, the "ought to have known" standard still applies**. This means that an offence section could require the Crown to prove that the principal offender had subjective intention or knowledge etc., but if that subjective *mens rea* isn't constitutionally required, the common intender could be liable on only the objective mental fault basis.

In ***R. v. Jackson***, [1993 CanLII 53 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1frvw){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada applies the test set out in ***Logan***. It found that because murder requires subjective mental fault under section 7 of the *Charter*, someone cannot be convicted as a party to a murder through common intention on an objective mental fault level (that they "ought to have known" the murder was a probable consequence). 

**However**, such an Accused could be convicted as a common intender of the lesser and included offence of manslaughter on an "ought to have known" standard because **manslaughter does not require subjective mental fault under section 7 of the *Charter***.
