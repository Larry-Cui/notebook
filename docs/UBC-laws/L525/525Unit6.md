#  Defences

<!-- #### <p class="highlight yellow" markdown="1">**Module Content**</p> -->

## 6.1 Introduction

Most defences “justify” or “excuse” behaviour that would otherwise have been considered criminal.

A common example is self-defence. A person who uses reasonable force to defend themselves against an assailant would not be found guilty of assault. **Self-defence is an example of a “true defence”** meaning that a successful Accused will be acquitted even if the Crown prosecutor proves beyond a reasonable doubt that the Accused committed the *actus reus* and *mens rea* of the offence.

Other defences are not “true defences” but instead negate the *actus reus* or *mens rea* of the offence. Examples include intoxication and NCR and these doctrines can be quite complex. In such cases, the defence prevents the Crown from proving its case, in contrast to true defences where Crown proves its case but the defence then leads to an acquittal.

<p class="highlight blue" markdown="1">**Study Tips**</p>

- Understand that some criminal defences **prevent** the Crown from proving the *actus reus* or *mens rea* of the offence. Other defences **apply after** the Crown has already proven its case. 
- For each of the defences we will study, consider: 
    - (1) at what stage does it operate? 
    - (2) what is the applicable onus/burden and standard of proof? 
    - (3) what are the essential elements of the defence? 
    - (4) are there any recognized constitutional standards or principles applicable to the defence? and 
    - (5) what is the outcome if the defence is successfully raised?

## 6.2 Mistake

**Mistake of Law**

A Mistake of Law occurs when an Accused doesn’t know that his or her actions constitute a criminal offence. S/he is unaware of the illegality of his or her conduct. 

Canadian courts do not recognize such “**ignorance of the law**” as a valid defence to criminal charges.

Ignorance of the law is **not a defence**, as codified in section 19 of the *Criminal Code*, which states:

!!! warning ""

    **s.19** Ignorance of the law by a person who commits an offence is not an excuse for committing that offence.

In other words, when establishing the *mens rea* of an offence, the Crown does NOT have to prove that the Accused knew s/he was committing an offence, or that what they were doing was illegal.

Mistakes of Law (ignorance of the law) is therefore not a defence in Canada. However, as with most things, there are a few narrow **exceptions** recognized in caselaw:

- **Non-publication:** A person may not be convicted of violating a law or regulation that has not been officially promulgated or published, it would be impossible to ascertain/comply with a law that is not yet available to the public.
- **Fault element:** There are certain rare offences that require some aspect of knowledge of the law (e.g. a legal error about who owns the property can be a defence to theft, “colour of right”). The key question here is **whether the essential element of the offence itself requires some degree of mental fault** on part of the Accused with respect to **the law or a legal right**. If so, the lack of such mental fault results in an acquittal because the Crown would not have proven the offence beyond a reasonable doubt.
- **Officially induced error:** which we will cover later in this topic

In ***R. v. Pamajewon***, [1996 CanLII 161 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1fr8z){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada discusses the difference between **mistake of law**, which is not a general defence, and **mistake of fact** which can exclude criminal responsibility.

**Mistake of Fact**

Mistake of Fact occurs when an Accused is mistaken about an essential factual element of the offence. The *mens rea* of the offence must require knowledge of the mistaken fact. 

A mistake of fact therefore prevents the Crown from proving its case because **it is unable to establish the required *mens rea***. Key to this analysis is to first determine what the mental fault of the offence is, and then to ask **whether the mistake of fact would negate that mental fault**.

A series of case excerpts explain in further detail the distinction between mistake of fact and mistake of law. They demonstrate how the distinction between mistake of fact and mistake of law can be tenuous, but how it remains **a significant dividing line** in our criminal law.

**Officially Induced Error**

In ***Lévis (City) v. Tétreault; Lévis (City) v. 2629-4470 Québec inc.***, [2006 SCC 12](https://canlii.ca/t/1n0zk){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada identified **six elements** to the defence of **Officially Induced Error**:

- An **error** of law or mixed fact/law must have been made
- The Accused considered the legal **consequences** of his/her actions
- The Accused obtained **advice from an appropriate official** (someone involved in the administration of the law)
- The advice from the official was **reasonable** in the circumstances (not a high threshold)
- The advice obtained was **erroneous**
- The Accused must show that they **relied on the advice**

The Court also held that it is **for the judge to determine** if Officially Induced Error operates in a given case as an excuse (e.g. not the jury). If a claim of officially induced error succeeds, this results in **a stay of proceedings, not an acquittal**. The Accused must establish the element of officially induced error, set out above, **on a balance of probabilities**. 

Officially Induced Error may apply to both **regulatory** and **criminal** offences.

## 6.3 Self-Defence

Self-defence is the justified use of force to defend oneself, one’s property, and certain third persons. It is **a statutory defence** in Canadian criminal law and there are numerous cases interpreting these provisions. Recently, however, some of these provisions were amended to expand the scope of the defence.

Self-defence is one of the most well-known defences. As noted earlier in this unit, it is a “**true defence**”, meaning that even if the Crown proves that the Accused committed the *actus reus* and *mens rea* of the offence beyond a reasonable doubt, the Accused can nevertheless be acquitted if they successfully raise self-defence.

**Introduction & The Codified Provisions**

This introduction explains the concept of self-defence and why it can be permissible for a person to use force to defend themselves. As you read these materials, think about some of the following questions:

- What are the **policy reasons** behind self-defence?
- Should self-defence **extend** only to threats to yourself? What about your property? Your children? Your partner? Your friend? Your dog? A stranger?
- Should **deadly force** in self-defence ever be permitted?
- How is self-defence different from **revenge** or being a **vigilante**?

**Air of Reality Test**

Caselaw interpreting the self-defence provisions in the former *Criminal Code* **section 34** held that self-defence should be considered (e.g. put to the jury in a jury trial or considered by the trial judge in a judge-only trial) if there is evidence that gives an “**air of reality**” to the defence, meaning:

> whether there is evidence on the record upon which a properly instructed jury acting reasonably could acquit if it believed the evidence to be true (***R. v. Cinous***, [2002 SCC 29](https://canlii.ca/t/51tb){:target=" \_blank"}, para. 92; emphasis added).

In ***Cinous***, the Supreme Court of Canada indicated that in determining whether the “air of reality test” is met, there is no assessment of credibility **at this stage** by the trial judge, **evidence must be assumed to be true**. 

However, “**mere assertions**” by the Accused that they were acting in self-defence are **insufficient**. If the Accused elaborates by testifying to specific facts that can form the basis for the evidence, then the air of reality test may be met.

If you want further explanation of the Air of Reality test in the context of self-defence, see the recent case of ***R v Mustard (G)***, [2016 MBCA 40](https://canlii.ca/t/gplth){:target=" \_blank"} at paragraphs 16 to 27 for a thorough summary.

**Statutory Provisions**

Read Sections 34 and 35 of the *Criminal Code*: Self-Defence Provisions

??? warning "an excerpt of sections 34 and 35 of the Criminal Code (it omits a few exceptions)"

    **Defence of Person**

    **Defence — use or threat of force**

    **34(1)** A person is not guilty of an offence if

    - (a) they **believe on reasonable grounds** that force is being used against them or another person or that a threat of force is being made against them or another person;
    - (b) the act that constitutes the offence is committed for the purpose of **defending or protecting** themselves or the other person from that use or threat of force; **and**
    - (c) the act committed is **reasonable** in the circumstances.

    **Factors**

    **(2)** In determining whether the act committed is reasonable in the circumstances, the court shall consider the relevant circumstances of the person, the other parties and the act, including, but not limited to, the following factors:

    - (a) the nature of the force or threat;
    - (b) the extent to which the use of force was imminent and whether there were other means available to respond to the potential use of force;
    - (c) the person’s role in the incident;
    - (d) whether any party to the incident used or threatened to use a weapon;
    - (e) the size, age, gender and physical capabilities of the parties to the incident;
    - (f) the nature, duration and history of any relationship between the parties to the incident, including any prior use or threat of force and the nature of that force or threat;
    - (f.1) any history of interaction or communication between the parties to the incident;
    - (g) the nature and proportionality of the person’s response to the use or threat of force; and
    - (h) whether the act committed was in response to a use or threat of force that the person knew was lawful.

    **Defence of Property**

    **Defence - property**

    **35(1)** A person is not guilty of an offence if

    - (a) they either believe on reasonable grounds that they are **in peaceable possession of property** or are acting under the authority of, or lawfully assisting, a person whom they believe on reasonable grounds is in peaceable possession of property;
    - (b) they believe on reasonable grounds that another person
        - (i) is about to enter, is entering or has entered the property without being entitled by law to do so,
        - (ii) is about to take the property, is doing so or has just done so, or
        - (iii) is about to damage or destroy the property, or make it inoperative, or is doing so;
    - (c) the act that constitutes the offence is **committed for the purpose of**
        - (i) preventing the other person from entering the property, or removing that person from the property, or
        - (ii) preventing the other person from taking, damaging or destroying the property or from making it inoperative, or retaking the property from that person; **and**
    - (d) the act committed is **reasonable in the circumstances**.

## 6.4 Necessity

Necessity involves urgent circumstances of clear and imminent peril where the Accused has no safe and legal avenue of escape. They **break the law out of necessity** and are subsequently excused from criminal liability.

This brief introduction explains the concept of necessity, which is **a controversial defence** that has only been recognized in Canada since a 1984 decision of the Supreme Court of Canada. There is **no statutory provision** in the *Criminal Code* recognizing necessity as it is **a purely common law defence**.  Consider why necessity is controversial and the reasons for why it is now a valid defence.

In ***Perka v. The Queen***, [1984 CanLII 23 (SCC)](https://canlii.ca/t/1lpfj){:target=" \_blank"}, the Supreme Court of Canada recognized necessity as a defence for the first time. The **essential elements** of necessity can be summarized from this decision as follows:

- **Urgency:** “at a minimum the situation must be so emergent and the peril so pressing that normal human instincts cry out for action and make a counsel of patience unreasonable”
- **No legal way out:** it must be “demonstrably impossible” to avoid the peril or prevent the harm without breaking the law
- **Proportionality:** the harm avoided must **exceed** the harm caused by the Accused to avoid it

The first two factors, (1) urgency and (2) lack of legal alternative are applied **subjectively** AND on **a modified objective standard**. The third factor (3) proportionality is applied on **a purely objective** standard. Recall:

- **Subjective Test**: What did this Accused actually himself think at the time?
- **Objective Test**: What would a reasonable person have thought (and/or done) at the time?  This requires the judge to set the standard of what "reasonable people" think and do in given situations.
- **Modified Objective Test**: What would a reasonable person have thought (and/or done) at the time, **if that person had the same knowledge, background, perceptions and capabilities as our Accused?**  This standard is also determined by the judge and straddles the middle ground between the subjective and objective tests.

Interestingly, in ***Perka***, Justice Dickson (as he then was) found that “**negligence or involvement in criminal or immoral activity does not disentitle the actor to the excuse of necessity**”. This could present a **controversial** set of circumstances, as seen in the following Discussion Activity.
