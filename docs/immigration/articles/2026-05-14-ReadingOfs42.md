# Reading of s. 42 of the *IRPA*

## Issues

Sections 34 to 40 of the [*Immigration and Refugee Protection Act*, SC 2001, c 27](https://canlii.ca/t/56cjs){:target="_blank"} set out seven grounds of inadmissibility: “**Security**” (s 34), “**Human or international rights violations**” (s 35), “**Serious criminality and criminality**” (s 36), “**Organized criminality**” (s 37), “**Health grounds**” (s 38), “**Financial reasons**” (s 39), and “**Misrepresentation**” (s 40).

These provisions apply directly to applicants. However, pursuant to s. 42 of the *IRPA*, inadmissibility may also extend to their accompanying or non-accompanying family members.

> 42 (1) A foreign national, other than a protected person, is inadmissible on grounds of an inadmissible family member if
> 
> - (a) their accompanying family member or, in prescribed circumstances, their non-accompanying family member is inadmissible; or
> - (b) they are an accompanying family member of an inadmissible person.
> 
> 42 (2) In the case of a foreign national referred to in subsection (1) who is a temporary resident or who has made an application for temporary resident status or an application to remain in Canada as a temporary resident,
> 
> - (a) the matters referred to in paragraph (1)(a) constitute inadmissibility only if the family member is inadmissible under section 34, 35, 35.1 or 37; and
> - (b) the matters referred to in paragraph (1)(b) constitute inadmissibility only if the foreign national is an accompanying family member of a person who is inadmissible under section 34, 35, 35.1 or 37.

This provision is generally self-explanatory. However, two issues remain to be addressed:

* Under s 42(1)(a), in what “prescribed circumstances” will the inadmissibility of a non-accompanying family member render the principal applicant inadmissible?

* Section 42(2) carves out ss 38–40 from the operation of family-member-based inadmissibility. Does this exception apply only to temporary resident applications, or does it also extend to permanent residence applications?

## Laws and Analysis

### Under s 42(1)(a), in what “prescribed circumstances” will the inadmissibility of a non-accompanying family member render the principal applicant inadmissible?

Section 23 of the [*Immigration and Refugee Protection Regulations, SOR/2002-227*](https://canlii.ca/t/56p02){:target="_blank"} defines the “prescribed circumstances”:

> 23 For the purposes of paragraph 42(1)(a) of the *Act*, the **prescribed circumstances** in which the foreign national is inadmissible on grounds of an inadmissible non-accompanying family member are that
> 
> - (a) the foreign national is a temporary resident or has made an application for temporary resident status, an application for a permanent resident visa or an application to remain in Canada as a temporary or permanent resident; and
> - (b) the non-accompanying family member is
>   - (i) the spouse of the foreign national, except where the relationship between the spouse and foreign national has broken down in law or in fact,
>   - (ii) the common-law partner of the foreign national,
>   - (iii) a dependent child of the foreign national and either the foreign national or an accompanying family member of the foreign national has custody of that child or is empowered to act on behalf of that child by virtue of a court order or written agreement or by operation of law, or
>   - (iv) a dependent child of a dependent child of the foreign national and the foreign national, a dependent child of the foreign national or any other accompanying family member of the foreign national has custody of that child or is empowered to act on behalf of that child by virtue of a court order or written agreement or by operation of law.

This is a notably broad definition, expanding the scope of inadmissibility in two respects. First, it applies in the context of both temporary and permanent residence applications. Second, it extends the concept of “family member” to include not only accompanying family members, but also non-accompanying spouses, common-law partners, dependent children, and dependent children of dependent children. This definition answers the first issue raised above.

The Court endorsed this interpretation in *Zhang v Canada (Citizenship and Immigration)*, [2012 FC 1093](https://canlii.ca/t/fsstt){:target="_blank"} at para 14.

> Thus, **regardless of whether or not the applicant actually planned to leave his son in China or not**, paragraph 42(a) of the *Act* extends the inadmissibility of the retarded son to the applicant. It follows that the other family members who are accompanying the applicant are also inadmissible by the effect of paragraph 42(b) of the *Act*.

A similar conclusion was reached in *Skobodzinska v Canada (Citizenship and Immigration)*, [2008 FC 887](https://canlii.ca/t/1zrrr){:target="_blank"} at para 19.

>  In the division of the *Act* relating to inadmissibility, s. 42(a) stipulates that a foreign national is inadmissible on grounds of **an inadmissible, non-accompanying family member** under prescribed circumstances. Those prescribed circumstances are laid out in s. 23 of the *Regulations*, the most relevant of which for the present case is s. 23(b)(iii) which indicates that a foreign national may be inadmissible if a non-accompanying family member is inadmissible ...

### Does section 42(2) apply only to temporary resident applications, or does it also extend to permanent residence applications?

To answer the second question concerning the scope of the s 42(2) “exception,” the wording of s 23 of the *IRPR* provides useful interpretive guidance. When the wording of s 42(2) of the *IRPA* is read alongside s 23 of the *IRPR*:

> s 23(a): the foreign national is a temporary resident or has made an application for temporary resident status, an application for a permanent resident visa or an application to remain in Canada as a temporary or permanent resident;

> s 42(2): In the case of a foreign national referred to in subsection (1) who is a temporary resident or who has made an application for temporary resident status or an application to remain in Canada as a temporary resident,

it becomes apparent that s 42(2) is limited to temporary resident applications and does not extend to permanent residence applications.

This interpretation has also been adopted in decisions of the Immigration and Refugee Board. In *Mak v Canada (Citizenship and Immigration)*, [2015 CanLII 80463 (CA IRB)](https://canlii.ca/t/gmf19){:target="_blank"} at para 8,

> ... The current provision 42(1) is a verbatim reproduction of the former section 42. The revision of the *Act* added **section 42(2) as an exception for temporary resident visa holders or applicants**. I find that the plain reading of the revised section is to create an exception in the specific case of temporary resident visas. **It makes no change to the inadmissibility provisions for permanent resident visa applicants**.

## Conclusion

Under s. 42(1)(a) of the *IRPA*, the inadmissibility of a non-accompanying family member will render the principal applicant inadmissible only in the “prescribed circumstances” set out in s. 23 of the *IRPR*. Those circumstances are broad and include both temporary and permanent residence applications, and extend to specific categories of non-accompanying family members—namely spouses (unless the relationship has broken down), common-law partners, dependent children under custody or legal authority, and certain dependent grandchildren. As confirmed in the jurisprudence, where these conditions are met, inadmissibility flows to the principal applicant even if the family member is not accompanying them.

With respect to s. 42(2), the provision operates as a clear limitation on family-member-based inadmissibility. It restricts the application of s. 42(1) in the context of temporary residents and temporary resident applicants, such that only security-related grounds under ss. 34, 35, 35.1, and 37 may trigger inadmissibility through family members. This exception does not extend to permanent residence applications. Accordingly, in permanent residence cases, the full scope of ss. 38 to 40 remains engaged for the purposes of s. 42(1).
