    Unit 5: Extensions of Criminal Liability

    5.2 Principal Offenders

    Study Tips

    • Go through the following terminology,
      • principal offender, party to an offence, aider, abetter, equally culpable, common intention, counselling an offence
    • Be able to explain in your own words why it is legally irrelevant whether someone directly committed an offence themselves, or whether they aided/abetted someone else to commit the offence.

    Principal offenders are described in section 21(1)(a) of the Criminal Code as the person who “actually commits” the offence. ...section 21(1) also identifies those who are a “party” to the offence by “aiding or abetting” the principal offender.

    The decision in R. v. Thatcher, 1987 CanLII 53 (SCC) shows the legal significance of section 21(1) of the Criminal Code, which makes the distinction between principal offenders versus aiders/abetters to an offence “legally irrelevant”, they are all “parties” to the offence, and thus “equally culpable”.

    ss 21, 22 of the Criminal Code

    Parties to offence

    21(1) Every one is a party to an offence who

    (a) actually commits it;

    (b) does or omits to do anything for the purpose of aiding any person to commit it; or

    (c) abets any person in committing it.

    Common intention

    (2) Where two or more persons form an intention in common to carry out an unlawful purpose and to assist each other therein and any one of them, in carrying out the common purpose, commits an offence, each of them who knew or ought to have known that the commission of the offence would be a probable consequence of carrying out the common purpose is a party to that offence.

    5.3 Aiding and Abetting

    Study Tips

    • What does it mean to “aid” someone in the context of s.21(1)? What does it mean to “abet”?
    • Map out how you would approach an exam question where an individual appears to be “merely present” at the scene of a crime. What factors would you consider in determining whether s.21(1) applies? What case law would you refer to, and what tests would you apply from those cases?
    • Know how both the actus reus and mens rea factor in to a s.21(1) analysis. Be able to approach a s.21(1) fact scenario with an examination of both the actus reus and the mens rea.

    “Aiding” refers to some form of “material assistance” (e.g. knowingly serving as a look-out or get-away driver during a robbery), whereas “abetting” refers to encouragement (e.g. verbal encouragement) to the person who “actually commits” the offence (i.e. the principal offender).

    Actus Reus of Aiding and Abetting

    R. v. Greyeyes introduced a new legal argument in drug cases called “Agent for the Purchaser”. Courts now apply the test set out in R. v. Greyeyes when dealing with such scenarios: did the Accused provided more that incidental assistance in facilitating the drug transaction? If so, then s/he can be found guilty under s.21(1) of aiding the offence of drug trafficking.

    In Dunlop and Sylvester, Justice Dickson (as he then was) for the majority held that:

    A person is not guilty merely because he is present at the scene of a crime and does nothing to prevent it.

    However, he added that:

    Presence at the commission of an offence can be evidence of aiding and abetting, if accompanied by other factors, such as prior knowledge of the principal offender’s intention to commit the offence or attendance for the purpose of encouragement.

    In R. v. Jackson, 2007 SCC 52, the Supreme Court of Canada returns to the issue of what role the presence of an Accused should have on the aiding/abetting analysis. As you read this case, apply the concepts set out in Dunlop and Sylvester, was Jackson merely present, or were there other factors at play?

    These brief excerpts from two supplementary cases identify the following additional principles/scenarios related to presence of the Accused and aiding/abetting:

    R. v. Nixon, 1990 CanLII 10993 (BC CA): In upholding the conviction, Justice Legg stated

    An accused who is present at the scene of an offence and who carries out no overt acts to aid or encourage the commission of the offence may nonetheless be convicted as a party if his purpose in failing to act was to aid in the commission of the offence.

    Factors relevant in this case included:

    • Presence of the Accused at the time of the assault
    • Knowledge of the assault
    • Statutory duty to protect the victim
    • Failure to act amounts to encouragement of the principal offender

    R. v. Popen, 1981 CanLII 3345 (ON CA):

    In some circumstances, a person who is present at the commission of an illegal act, which he has a duty to prevent, may by mere inactivity encourage the illegal act.

    Mens Rea of Aiding and Abetting

    Recall that Section 21(1) of the Criminal Code encompasses anyone who “does or omits to do anything for the purpose of aiding any person to commit [an offence]” (emphasis added). The following decisions consider what level of mental fault this requires.

    R. v. Briscoe, 2010 SCC 13

    [16] The mens rea requirement reflected in the word “purpose” under s. 21(1)(b) has two components: intent and knowledge. For the intent component, it was settled in R. v. Hibbert, [1995] 2 S.C.R. 973, that “purpose” in s. 21(1)(b) should be understood as essentially synonymous with “intention”. It is therefore not required that the accused desired that the offence be successfully committed (Hibbert, at para. 35).

    [17] As for knowledge, in order to have the intention to assist in the commission of an offence, the aider must know that the perpetrator intends to commit the crime, although he or she need not know precisely how it will be committed. That sufficient knowledge is a prerequisite for intention is simply a matter of common sense.

    Discussion: 5.3 R v Dunlop & Sylvester

    Answer

    In Dunlop and Sylvester v. The Queen, 1979 CanLII 20 (SCC), the court held that “mere presence at the scene of a crime is not sufficient to ground culpability.” Liability requires both the actus reus and mens rea components.

    For actus reus, “it requires some encouragement of the principal offender: an act which facilitates the commission of the offence, such as keeping watch or enticing the victim away, or an act which tends to prevent or hinder interference with accomplishment of the criminal act, such as preventing the intended victim from escaping or being ready to assist the prime culprit.”

    For mens rea, the court held that “presence at the commission of an offence can be evidence of aiding and abetting if accompanied by other factors, such as prior knowledge of the principal offender’s intention to commit the offence or attendance for the purpose of encouragement. ... A person cannot properly be convicted of aiding or abetting in the commission of acts which he does not know may be or are intended...”

    There is, however, one situation in which “mere presence” may be considered a form of encouragement for an offence.

    In R. v. Nixon, 1990 CanLII 10993 (BC CA), the court held that “[B]ut assuming there was only mere presence, this case has one very important aspect that was not present in ... the Dunlop and Sylvester v. The Queen case. That is the fact that Nixon [the accused] is a police officer and has the obligation to protect his charges. He has that duty by legislation. ... He was there, he had a duty to involve himself.”

    The court concluded that “it has been held to be sufficient that the accused had the power to control the acts of the person committing the offence. Where such control exists a failure to act has been interpreted to constitute encouragement.”

    Samantha Comment

    The main take-away from this case is that an accused cannot be considered to have Aided & Abetted in a crime simply by just being present. Per Dickson, J. “Mere presence at the scene of the crime is not sufficient to ground culpability. something more is needed.”

    Several examples are given in the decision for what that “something more” could be:

    • Encouragement of the principal offender,
    • Keeping watch,
    • Enticing the victim,
    • Preventing the victim form escaping,
    • Being on the ready to assist the principle offender if needed,
    • Omitting to do something it is one’s duty to do

    There is still an actus reus and a mens rea component even if someone is charged with an offence as an accessory under s.21(1). It is this “something more” that will be the actus reus of the aiding and/or abetting. The Crown would still have to show that the actus reus of a rape took place (even if by someone else). In the case of a rape, the mens rea would be the same as that of the principal offender: knowledge that un-consensual intercourse was taking place.

    Discussion: 5.3 R v Greyeyes

    Answer

    I agree with the majority’s approach in R v Greyeyes.

    Faced with the “agent of the purchaser” defence, the court first explained that “to abet within the meaning of s. 21(1)(c) includes encouraging, instigating, promoting or procuring the crime to be committed.” Promoting or procuring the crime to be committed seems to be a fairly broad definition.

    The court then confirmed that “a purchaser should not, by reason of the purchase alone, be found to be a party to the offence of trafficking”: Poitras v. R., 1973 CanLII 156 (SCC).

    This case illustrates how the court looked beyond the label of “agent” and instead focused on the actual conduct of the accused and its real-world effect on the commission of the offence.

    Samantha Comment

    The test that Greyeyes got boiled down to is whether the accused "provided more than incidental assistance" to the buyer in making the purchase.

    It seems as though the majority are concerned with moral culpability in a way. Someone might assist in drug trafficking such that the actus reus and the mens rea are made out, yet still not be morally blameworthy enough to warrant a criminal sanction. They don't use these words, but it seems this is what influences the majority decision.

    I think that Cory J is right strictly speaking, that the definition of Aiding and Abetting would include even minor assistance in the sale. Morally though, I would personally find it hard to justify culpability for trafficking when one is involved in a trivial way. Especially considering that non-custodial Conditional Sentence Orders are no longer available for trafficking in hard drugs, and that trafficking can attract a 1 or 2 year mandatory minimum sentence in certain situations.

    Keep in mind that both the majority and the minority reached the same result for the particular set of facts in the Greyeyes case, that Greyeyes did indeed aid and abet in this trafficking. The difference between the two camps is that the majority set a higher threshold for aiding and abetting in a drug trafficking case, there must be more than incidental assistance. The minority set a lower threshold for culpability, whether the accused assisted or encouraged the sale.

    Discussion: 5.3 R v Jackson

    Samantha Comment

    This is an interesting case for the concept of Aiding. The key to the Jackson case is that he was not merely present at the scene of the marijuana grow operation. There was also some evidence that he was actively involved in the production as a principal participant:

    • he was present at the secluded location (as in Dunlop, this is one piece of evidence, but not determinative)
    • he had been there for at least 2 days
    • 5 people were involved and 5 were present
    • he was sleeping in a tent on site
    • there was grow equipment in the tent
    • he was wearing rubber boots
    • there was nothing else there other than the grow

    What you don't see in your text is a discussion of the law on inferences. The Crown lawyer in Jackson would have asked the judge to draw the inference that Jackson was a party to the grow operation based on the evidence before the court (summarized above). The inference in this case is that Jackson must have been involved in the grow operation because there is no evidence before the court to support any other conclusion.

    A judge can base a conviction on an inference if it is the only reasonable inference that can be made on the facts in evidence. This comes into play in paragraph 10 of Jackson: "it was open to the trial judge to concluded, as he did, that the appellant's presence at the scene of the crime was consistent only with his culpable involvement."

    So the Jackson case is consistent with Dunlop because there was something more at play than just mere presence. There was a body of evidence that lead to only one reasonable inference, that Jackson was a party to the offence.

    5.4 Common Intention

    Common intention expands the net of criminal responsibility beyond the principal offender, co-principals (if any), aiders/abetters (if any), to persons who do not actually assist or encourage the commission of the offence for which they are charged as a party.

    Common Intention: s.21(2) of the Criminal Code

    21(2) Where two or more persons

    • form an intention in common
    • to carry out an unlawful purpose and to assist each other therein
    • and any one of them, in carrying out the common purpose, commits an offence
    • each of them who knew or ought to have known that the commission of the offence would be a probable consequence of carrying out the common purpose

    is a party to that offence.

    The Supreme Court of Canada decision in R. v. Kirkness, 1990 CanLII 57 (SCC) recognizes a number of key principles with respect to common intention.

    • common intention need not be pre-planned in any way. it is sufficient that it arise[s] just prior to or at the time of the commission of the offence.
    • The essential elements of common intention are usually "implied from the facts"
    • Where one has aided or abetted in the commission of an offence, there can be little doubt that a shared intention to effect an unlawful purpose existed
    • To abandon participation in the unlawful purpose, "abandonment" (which can be a defence for common intention) requires "something more than a mere mental change of intention and physical change of place; where practicable and reasonable there must be timely communication of the intention to abandon the common purpose to those who desire to continue it; defendants will be held to a different standard depending upon the degree of participation in the crime
    • A causal, but not a temporal connection, is required between the unlawful act and the ultimate offence

    Constitutional Issues with Common Intention

    In R. v. Logan, 1990 CanLII 84 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada considered the constitutional validity of the "ought to have known" aspect of common intention.

    It found that it is a constitutional requirement under s.7 of the Charter for some offences to have subjective mens rea. This is due to the seriousness of those offences and the stigma involved in their conviction.

    However, for other offences, the "ought to have known" standard still applies. This means that an offence section could require the Crown to prove that the principal offender had subjective intention or knowledge etc., but if that subjective mens rea isn't constitutionally required, the common intender could be liable on only the objective mental fault basis.

    In R. v. Jackson, 1993 CanLII 53 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada applies the test set out in Logan. It found that because murder requires subjective mental fault under section 7 of the Charter, someone cannot be convicted as a party to a murder through common intention on an objective mental fault level (that they "ought to have known" the murder was a probable consequence).

    However, such an Accused could be convicted as a common intender of the lesser and included offence of manslaughter on an "ought to have known" standard because manslaughter does not require subjective mental fault under section 7 of the Charter.

    Discussion: 5.4 R v Logan

    Samantha Comment

    What you need to take away from Jackson and Dunlop and Logan and this line of cases is that something more than mere presence is needed to base a conviction.

    ... in a fact pattern situation, you want to look through and pick out all the pieces of evidence that are "something more" then look at them in their totality to determine if they amount to Aid & Abet.

    5.5 Attempts

    Mental fault is the dominant feature of attempts and the main issue is typically whether the conduct of the Accused has gone far enough to constitute an attempted crime.

    Let us break s.24(1) of the Criminal Code down and look more closely at its individual components.

    24(1)

    Every one who,

    • having an intent to commit an offence (mens rea)
    • does or omits to do anything (actus reus)
    • for the purpose of carrying out the intention (mens rea, remember how this phraseology means the same as “intention”)

    is guilty of an attempt to commit the offence

    • whether or not it was possible under the circumstances to commit the offence (we will deal with impossibilities later in this Topic)

    A key issue that we will explore in this section is when an act or omission has gone beyond “mere preparation” or is “too remote to constitute an attempt.”

    Note how the penalties for attempted crimes are generally a reduced version of what the Accused would have incurred as a sentence if the crime had been completed.

    Mens Rea for Attempts

    Section 24(1) of the Criminal Code states that to be liable for an attempt the Accused must intend to commit the offence, and their conduct must be for the purpose of carrying out this intention. This subjective mens rea component is at the heart of an attempts analysis. Did the Accused have a specific intention to carry out the full offence? This analysis requires an examination of the surrounding circumstances and often the drawing of inferences about what the Accused was thinking.

    Actus Reus for Attempts

    An intention to commit an offence is not enough, by itself, to support a conviction under s.24(1). There must also be some external conduct whereby the Accused acts on that intention.

    Deutsch v. The Queen, 1986 CanLII 21 (SCC) is the leading case setting out the test for the whether an Accused has committed the actus reus of an attempt*.

    It is key to this analysis that we first understand what the actus reus is for the completed offence. Then, to determine whether the Accused is liable for attempting the offence, one must analyze the relationship between the nature and quality of the act in question (i.e. the conduct of the Accused) and the nature of the completed offence.

    In doing so, the Supreme Court of Canada says we are to consider the relative proximity of the acts of the Accused in relation to the completed offence (i.e. in terms of time, location, and the things the Accused still had left to do). Note the following passages from this case.

    As with any other crime, the Crown must prove a mens rea, that is, the intent to commit the offence in question and the actus reus, that is, some step towards the commission of the offence attempted going beyond mere acts of preparation. Of the two elements the more significant is the mens rea.

    ... but when that preparation is in fact fully completed, the next step in the series of acts done by the accused for the purpose and with the intention of committing the crime as planned cannot, in my opinion, be regarded as remote in its connection with that crime. The connection is in fact proximate.

    Impossibility and Attempts

    section 24(2) of the Criminal Code

    Every one who, having an intent to commit an offence, does or omits to do anything for the purpose of carrying out his intention is guilty of an attempt to commit the offence whether or not it was possible under the circumstances to commit the offence.

    Factual impossibility exits where an Accused intends to commit an offence but is prevented from doing so because of some factual circumstance (ie, goes to rob a bank but the bank is closed for the day and all locked up). Legal impossibility occurs where the “criminal activity” the Accused intends to do is, unknown to them, not actually illegal because of a state of law (ie, an Accused tries to steal something that s/he actually already owns).

    There is another category of impossibilities referred to as “Imaginary Crimes”. These acts, perceived by the Accused as being criminal, are not actually criminal acts at all. It is therefore irrelevant whether the Accused intends to commit the act, as the act is not illegal to perform.

    Discussion: 5.5 Attempts & Impossibilities

    Answer

    The issue in United States of America v. Dynar, 1997 CanLII 359 (SCC), is whether the respondent’s conduct in the United States would constitute a crime if carried out in Canada, thereby meeting the requirement of “double criminality,” which is a precondition for extradition.

    Because the accused did not complete the offence due to impossibility, a further issue arose concerning the scope of liability for attempted offences under Canadian criminal law, specifically, whether impossibility of completion constitutes a defence to a charge of attempt or conspiracy in Canada.

    In this case, the Court grappled with different categories of criminal impossibility. According to the Court, “the only relevant distinction for purposes of s. 24(1) of the Criminal Code is between imaginary crimes and attempts to do the factually impossible.

    The criminal law of Canada recognizes no middle category called “legal impossibility.”

    The Court also disregarded differences in the circumstances that caused the factual impossibility and concluded that impossibility of execution is never a defence.

    Accordingly, the Court held that “s. 24(1) draws no distinction between attempts to do the possible but by inadequate means, attempts to do the physically impossible, and attempts to do something that turns out to be impossible “following completion.” All are varieties of attempts to do the “factually impossible” and all are crimes.”

    On the other hand, imaginary crimes refer to acts that the accused perceives to be criminal but that are not actually criminal offences. The Court confirmed that “attempts to commit imaginary crimes fall outside the scope of the provision [s. 24(1)].”

    Unit 6: Defences

    Self-defence is an example of a “true defence” meaning that a successful Accused will be acquitted even if the Crown prosecutor proves beyond a reasonable doubt that the Accused committed the actus reus and mens rea of the offence.

    Other defences are not “true defences” but instead negate the actus reus or mens rea of the offence. Examples include intoxication and NCR and these doctrines can be quite complex. In such cases, the defence prevents the Crown from proving its case, in contrast to true defences where Crown proves its case but the defence then leads to an acquittal.

    6.2 Mistake

    Mistake of Law

    A Mistake of Law occurs when an Accused doesn’t know that his or her actions constitute a criminal offence. S/he is unaware of the illegality of his or her conduct.

    Canadian courts do not recognize such “ignorance of the law” as a valid defence to criminal charges. Ignorance of the law is not a defence.

    Section 19 of the Criminal Code

    Ignorance of the law by a person who commits an offence is not an excuse for committing that offence.

    However, as with most things, there are a few narrow exceptions recognized in case law:

    • Non-publication: A person may not be convicted of violating a law or regulation that has not been officially promulgated or published, it would be impossible to ascertain/comply with a law that is not yet available to the public.
    • Fault element: There are certain rare offences that require some aspect of knowledge of the law (e.g. a legal error about who owns the property can be a defence to theft, “colour of right”). The key question here is whether the essential element of the offence itself requires some degree of mental fault on part of the Accused with respect to the law or a legal right. If so, the lack of such mental fault results in an acquittal because the Crown would not have proven the offence beyond a reasonable doubt.
    • Officially induced error: which we will cover later in this topic

    Mistake of Fact

    Mistake of Fact occurs when an Accused is mistaken about an essential factual element of the offence. The mens rea of the offence must require knowledge of the mistaken fact.

    A mistake of fact therefore prevents the Crown from proving its case because it is unable to establish the required mens rea. Key to this analysis is to first determine what the mental fault of the offence is, and then to ask whether the mistake of fact would negate that mental fault.

    In R. v. Pamajewon, 1996 CanLII 161 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada discusses the difference between mistake of law, which is not a general defence, and mistake of fact which can exclude criminal responsibility.

    Discussion Forum: Mistake of Fact vs. Mistake of Law?

    Answer

    Ignorance or mistake of the law is not a defence. As s. 19 of the Criminal Code states: “Ignorance of the law by a person who commits an offence is not an excuse for committing that offence.” When establishing the mens rea of an offence, the Crown does not need to prove that the accused acted out of ignorance or mistake of the law.

    There are, however, a few narrow exceptions to this rule, including: where a law has not been made publicly available, where knowledge of the law is an essential element of the offence, and in cases of officially induced error.

    Mistake of fact, on the other hand, may raise a reasonable doubt as to the mental fault element and thus prevent the Crown from proving mens rea. Once properly established, a mistake of fact can operate as a valid defence.

    In Scenario 2, the accused knew he was trafficking a prohibited substance. He claimed he believed it to be heroin, but it was later determined to be fentanyl. This assertion should not be accepted at face value; rather, it must be assessed in light of all surrounding evidence. If the Crown can establish beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused knew the substance was fentanyl, then the defence amounts, at most, to a mistake of law and should be rejected. If, however, the Crown cannot do so, the accused is entitled to the benefit of the doubt, and the mistake of fact defence should succeed. In that case, the accused should be convicted of the lesser offence.

    Officially Induced Error

    In Lévis (City) v. Tétreault; Lévis (City) v. 2629-4470 Québec inc., 2006 SCC 12, the Supreme Court of Canada identified six elements to the defence of Officially Induced Error:

    • An error of law or mixed fact/law must have been made
    • The Accused considered the legal consequences of his/her actions
    • The Accused obtained advice from an appropriate official (someone involved in the administration of the law)
    • The advice from the official was reasonable in the circumstances (not a high threshold)
    • The advice obtained was erroneous
    • The Accused must show that they relied on the advice

    The Accused must establish the element of officially induced error, set out above, on a balance of probabilities.

    6.3 Self-Defence

    Self-defence is the justified use of force to defend oneself, one’s property, and certain third persons.

    Statutory Provisions

    an excerpt of sections 34 and 35 of the Criminal Code (it omits a few exceptions)

    Defence of Person

    Defence — use or threat of force

    34(1) A person is not guilty of an offence if

    • (a) they believe on reasonable grounds that force is being used against them or another person or that a threat of force is being made against them or another person;
    • (b) the act that constitutes the offence is committed for the purpose of defending or protecting themselves or the other person from that use or threat of force; and
    • (c) the act committed is reasonable in the circumstances.

    Factors

    (2) In determining whether the act committed is reasonable in the circumstances, the court shall consider the relevant circumstances of the person, the other parties and the act, including, but not limited to, the following factors:

    • (a) the nature of the force or threat;
    • (b) the extent to which the use of force was imminent and whether there were other means available to respond to the potential use of force;
    • (c) the person’s role in the incident;
    • (d) whether any party to the incident used or threatened to use a weapon;
    • (e) the size, age, gender and physical capabilities of the parties to the incident;
    • (f) the nature, duration and history of any relationship between the parties to the incident, including any prior use or threat of force and the nature of that force or threat;
    • (f.1) any history of interaction or communication between the parties to the incident;
    • (g) the nature and proportionality of the person’s response to the use or threat of force; and
    • (h) whether the act committed was in response to a use or threat of force that the person knew was lawful.

    Air of Reality Test

    Caselaw interpreting the self-defence provisions in the former Criminal Code section 34 held that self-defence should be considered (e.g. put to the jury in a jury trial or considered by the trial judge in a judge-only trial) if there is evidence that gives an “air of reality” to the defence, meaning:

    whether there is evidence on the record upon which a properly instructed jury acting reasonably could acquit if it believed the evidence to be true (R. v. Cinous, 2002 SCC 29, para. 92; emphasis added).

    In Cinous, the Supreme Court of Canada indicated that in determining whether the “air of reality test” is met, there is no assessment of credibility at this stage by the trial judge, evidence must be assumed to be true.

    However, “mere assertions” by the Accused that they were acting in self-defence are insufficient. If the Accused elaborates by testifying to specific facts that can form the basis for the evidence, then the air of reality test may be met.

    Discussion Forum: Self-Defence Practice Problem

    Answer

    For the charge of second-degree murder, the accused (a 20-year-old woman) has a valid claim of self-defence. However, this defence is not available in relation to the charge of possession of a prohibited weapon (i.e., the taser).

    Section 34(1) of the Criminal Code provides that a person is not guilty of an offence if “force or a threat of force is being used against them,” they commit the act “for the purpose of defending or protecting themselves from that use or threat of force,” and “the act committed is reasonable.”

    Here, the accused was confronted by a male robber in a parking lot and used the taser she carried in her pocket to protect herself. The threat and purpose requirements of self-defence are clearly satisfied. Although she deployed the taser for 30 seconds, resulting in the robber’s death, the use of the weapon still falls within the scope of reasonableness.

    Section 34(2) sets out a non-exhaustive list of factors to be considered in assessing whether the act was reasonable. The most relevant factors in this case include the nature of the force or threat, whether other means of responding were available, the size, gender, and physical capabilities of the parties, as well as the nature and proportionality of the response.

    It is significant that the incident occurred at midnight and that the nearest source of help, a parking attendant, was approximately 100 metres away. The accused was a 20-year-old young woman who was physically overmatched by a male assailant threatening force. In those circumstances, there were no realistic alternatives available to her other than using the taser to fully subdue the attacker. Considering all of these circumstances, her actions were reasonable, and she should be acquitted of the second-degree murder charge.

    However, with respect to the charge of possession of a prohibited weapon, the accused has no viable self-defence argument. The possession of the taser constituted an ongoing legal status that arose long before the parking lot incident and was not undertaken in response to any immediate force or threat of force. Accordingly, the accused should be convicted on that charge.

    6.4 Necessity

    Necessity involves urgent circumstances of clear and imminent peril where the Accused has no safe and legal avenue of escape. They break the law out of necessity and are subsequently excused from criminal liability.

    In Perka v. The Queen, 1984 CanLII 23 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada recognized necessity as a defence for the first time. The essential elements of necessity can be summarized from this decision as follows:

    • Urgency: “at a minimum the situation must be so emergent and the peril so pressing that normal human instincts cry out for action and make a counsel of patience unreasonable”
    • No legal way out: it must be “demonstrably impossible” to avoid the peril or prevent the harm without breaking the law
    • Proportionality: the harm avoided must exceed the harm caused by the Accused to avoid it

    The first two factors, (1) urgency and (2) lack of legal alternative are applied subjectively AND on a modified objective standard. The third factor (3) proportionality is applied on a purely objective standard. Recall:

    • Subjective Test: What did this Accused actually himself think at the time?
    • Objective Test: What would a reasonable person have thought (and/or done) at the time? This requires the judge to set the standard of what "reasonable people" think and do in given situations.
    • Modified Objective Test: What would a reasonable person have thought (and/or done) at the time, if that person had the same knowledge, background, perceptions and capabilities as our Accused? This standard is also determined by the judge and straddles the middle ground between the subjective and objective tests.

    Interestingly, in Perka, Justice Dickson (as he then was) found that “negligence or involvement in criminal or immoral activity does not disentitle the actor to the excuse of necessity”. This could present a controversial set of circumstances, as seen in the following Discussion Activity.

    Discussion Activity: Necessity Practice Problems

    Answer

    Scenario #2

    Necessity as a defence means that when the accused is confronted with a clear and imminent peril, and has no legal or safe way out, they may break the law out of necessity and be excused from criminal liability.

    This defence is not codified in legislation, but rather arises from the common law. It was first recognized by the Supreme Court of Canada in Perka v. The Queen, 1984 CanLII 23 (SCC). The essential elements of the defence of necessity can be summarized as follows:

    • Urgency: the situation faced by the accused must be emergent, and the peril must be so pressing that a counsel of patience would be unreasonable.
    • No legal way out: it must be “demonstrably impossible” to avoid the peril or prevent the harm without breaking the law.
    • Proportionality: the harm avoided must outweigh the harm caused by the accused in avoiding it.

    The first two elements are assessed on a modified objective standard, while the third element is assessed on a purely objective standard.

    The modified objective standard asks what a reasonable person would have thought and/or done at the time if that person possessed the same knowledge, background, perceptions, and capabilities as the accused.

    In Scenario 2, when the accused arrived at the house, he was surrounded by 20 to 40 people. Given the threatening calls that had previously been exchanged between the accused and the person in possession of the beer keg, it was reasonable for the accused to believe that these individuals intended to harm him at the scene. In a matter of seconds, a reasonable person in the same circumstances would likely prioritize escape over attempting to negotiate a peaceful resolution. Therefore, the urgency and no legal way out elements are satisfied on these facts.

    However, the accused may fail on the third element, proportionality. The mere fact that he was surrounded by 20 or more people does not necessarily mean that he would have been beaten or injured. By contrast, when two men were struck and injured as he reversed the vehicle, the harm actually caused may have exceeded the harm he was attempting to avoid.

    In conclusion, the accused likely does not have a successful defence of necessity to the charge of dangerous operation of a motor vehicle.

    6.5 Duress

    Duress is a similar defence to necessity, except in the case of duress, the threat or compulsion comes from another person as opposed to circumstances. Duress is more complex, however, because it has a different statutory and common law manifestation depending on whether the Accused is a principal offender or a party.

    Duress in Statute

    This brief introduction explains the defence of duress, which is both a statutory defence and a common law defence. It is also a defence that the Accused must raise an “air of reality” in order for it to be considered.

    Section 17 of the Criminal Code

    A person who commits an offence

    • under compulsion by threats of immediate death or bodily harm
    • from a person who is present when the offence is committed

    is excused for committing the offence if

    • the person believes that the threats will be carried out and
    • if the person is not a party to a conspiracy or association whereby the person is subject to compulsion,

    but this section does not apply where the offence that is committed is high treason or treason, murder, piracy, attempted murder, sexual assault, sexual assault with a weapon, threats to a third party or causing bodily harm, aggravated sexual assault, forcible abduction, hostage taking, robbery, assault with a weapon or causing bodily harm, aggravated assault, unlawfully causing bodily harm, arson or an offence under sections 280 to 283 (abduction and detention of young persons).

    Section 17 of the Criminal Code applies only to principal offenders (see Paquette v. R., 1976 CanLII 24 (SCC)), whereas parties to an offence (e.g. aiders and abettors, common intenders) can only claim duress as a common law defence.

    According to R. v. Hibbert, 1995 CanLII 110 (SCC), duress does not negate mens rea, but may result in a finding of moral involuntariness that operates as a defence.

    Constitutional Issues

    In R. v. Ruzic, 2001 SCC 24, the Supreme Court of Canada considered a constitutional challenge under section 7 of the Charter to the statutory defence of duress in section 17 of the Criminal Code. The Court held that it is unjust to penalize a person who acted in a morally involuntary fashion because his acts cannot be reasonably attributed to him, his will was constrained by another force.

    The Court held that the “immediacy” and “presence” requirements for duress under section 17 of the Criminal Code infringed section 7 of the Charter.

    Duress at Common Law

    As with Perka, each element of the common law defence of duress must be both subjectively held by the Accused AND found to be objectively reasonable.

    The B.C. Supreme Court explained the common law test for duress as follows:

    R. v. Poon and Wong, 2006 BCSC 1158

    [3] In R. v. Cinous, 2002 SCC 29 at para. 60, the Court articulated this test as follows:

    [T]he trial judge must determine if the evidence put forward is such that, if believed, a reasonable jury properly charged could have acquitted.

    The onus then shifts to the Crown to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused was not acting under duress.

    [4] The elements of the defence of duress are set out in Ruzic as follows:

    • (1) the accused acts solely as a result of threats of death, or serious bodily harm to him or herself or another person;
    • (2) the threats were of such gravity or seriousness that the accused person believed that the threats would be carried out;
    • (3) the threats were of such gravity that they might well have caused a reasonable person, placed in the same situation as the accused, to act in the same manner as he or she did; in other words, would a person of reasonable firmness, sharing the characteristics of the accused, such as his or her age and his or her background, have responded to the threats; and
    • (4) the accused must not have had an obvious safe avenue of escape.

    [5] The accused is not required to seek the protection of the police in every circumstance.

    [6] Subsequent cases have emphasized the need for the jury to assess the proportionality of the accused’s response, again, on a subjective/objective analysis, and the requirement for the accused to show reasonable fortitude in resisting the threats: R. v. Williams, 2002 BCCA 453.

    [7] A recognized exception to the defence of duress was considered in R. v. Li, 2002 CanLII 18077 (ON CA). Li stands for the proposition that voluntary involvement in criminal activity negates both the common law and statutory defence of duress.

    Duress operates as a true defence. As touched on in the above passage, Crown must first prove that the Accused committed the underlying offence. The Accused may then assert duress based on evidence presented in either the Crown or the Defence case at trial. If there is an air of reality to the assertion, the defence of duress will be put to the jury, and the onus of proof will shift back to the Crown to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the Accused was not acting under duress.

    Comparing Both Forms of Duress

    R. v. Ryan, 2013 SCC 3

    [81] The defence of duress, in its statutory and common law forms, is largely the same. The two forms share the following common elements:

    • There must be an explicit or implicit threat of present or future death or bodily harm. This threat can be directed at the accused or a third party.
    • The accused must reasonably believe that the threat will be carried out.
    • There is no safe avenue of escape. This element is evaluated on a modified objective standard.
    • A close temporal connection between the threat and the harm threatened.
    • Proportionality between the harm threatened and the harm inflicted by the accused. The harm caused by the accused must be equal to or no greater than the harm threatened. This is also evaluated on a modified objective standard.
    • The accused is not a party to a conspiracy or association whereby the accused is subject to compulsion and actually knew that threats and coercion to commit an offence were a possible result of this criminal activity, conspiracy or association.

    [82] Certain differences remain.

    [83] The first is that, as was established in Paquette and confirmed in Ruzic, the statutory defence applies to principals, while the common law defence is available to parties to an offence. The second is that the statutory version of the defence has a lengthy list of exclusions, whereas it is unclear in the Canadian common law of duress whether any offences are excluded. This results in the rather incoherent situation that principals who commit one of the enumerated offences cannot rely on the defence of duress while parties to those same offences, however, can.

    Discussion Activity: Excluded Offence for Statutory Defence of Duress

    Answer

    There are two aspects of s. 17 of the Criminal Code that have raised constitutional concerns: the “immediacy” and “presence” requirements, and the exclusion of certain offences from the defence of duress.

    The first aspect was declared unconstitutional by the Court in R. v. Ruzic, 2001 SCC 24. However, with respect to the second aspect, the exclusion of certain offences, the legal reality remains that an accused may still be convicted even if they acted under duress. I agree with this framework and do not believe that the exclusion of certain offences under s. 17 violates the Charter.

    In R. v. Ryan, 2013 SCC 3, the Court held that the statutory duress defence applies to principals, while the common law defence remains available to parties to an offence. Although the Court acknowledged that this is an unsatisfactory state of the law, the practical result is that principals who commit one of the excluded offences cannot rely on the statutory defence of duress, whereas parties to those same offences may still invoke the common law defence.

    As I see it, the rationale for this exclusion is that the excluded offences are largely violent crimes or require active operations. A person who commits such crimes under duress will often be more accurately characterized not as the principal offender, but as an aider or party to the offence. Admittedly, in extreme circumstances, the accused may still be the principal, or one of several principals. However, as the Court stated in R. v. Poon and Wong, 2006 BCSC 1158, the criminal law expects the accused to demonstrate reasonable fortitude in resisting threats. Acting as a principal is an important factor suggesting voluntary participation in criminal activity, which undermines both the common law and statutory defences of duress.

    6.6 Intoxication

    There are two types of intoxication for our purposes: involuntary and voluntary (self-induced).

    Involuntary intoxication can negate an Accused's mens rea or make their actions physically involuntary. The defence of involuntary intoxication can be applied to any charges.

    Self-induced intoxication is a controversial potential defence that only applies in certain circumstances. We will focus on self-induced intoxication in this topic, which is a mixture of statutory provisions and common law decisions.

    Self-Induced Intoxication

    From the case of R. v. Daley, 2007 SCC 53 which we will read later in this Topic:

    [35] Courts in England and Canada have taken the reference in Beard to "forming the specific intent essential to constitute the crime" as drawing a distinction between crimes of specific intent and those of general intent, such that the defence of intoxication is traditionally only available with respect to the former.

    Specific intent crimes are those where the Accused specifically intends the result.

    General intent crimes, on the other hand, require only that the Accused intended to do the actus reus itself. S/he does not need to intend what results from the act, such as death in the case of manslaughter.

    Self-induced intoxication can prevent the Crown from establishing the requisite mens rea in specific intent offences. For general intent crimes, self-induced intoxication may be used as a defence only if the offence does NOT include "as an element an assault or any other interference or threat of interference by a person with the bodily integrity of another person," per s.33.1 of the Criminal Code.

    Section 33.1 of the Criminal Code

    Offences of violence by negligence

    33.1 (1) A person who, by reason of self-induced extreme intoxication, lacks the general intent or voluntariness ordinarily required to commit an offence referred to in subsection (3), nonetheless commits the offence if

    • (a) all the other elements of the offence are present; and

    • (b) before they were in a state of extreme intoxication, they departed markedly from the standard of care expected of a reasonable person in the circumstances with respect to the consumption of intoxicating substances.

    Marked departure — foreseeability of risk and other circumstances

    (2) For the purposes of determining whether the person departed markedly from the standard of care, the court must consider the objective foreseeability of the risk that the consumption of the intoxicating substances could cause extreme intoxication and lead the person to harm another person. The court must, in making the determination, also consider all relevant circumstances, including anything that the person did to avoid the risk.

    Offences

    (3) This section applies in respect of an offence under this Act or any other Act of Parliament that includes as an element an assault or any other interference or threat of interference by a person with the bodily integrity of another person.

    Definition of extreme intoxication

    (4) In this section, extreme intoxication means intoxication that renders a person unaware of, or incapable of consciously controlling, their behaviour.

    Section 33.1 is a key statutory provision that limits the availability of the defence of self-induced intoxication for general intent offences involving threats or acts of violence.

    In the leading case of R. v. Daley, 2007 SCC 53, the Supreme Court of Canada sets out the current scope of the defence of self-induced intoxication, including the three legally relevant levels of intoxication and their application to specific versus general intent offences.

    R. v. Daley, 2007 SCC 53, headnote (edited, with emphasis)

    • On the current state of the law, for a murder charge, the defence of intoxication will only be available to negate specific intent so as to reduce the charge to manslaughter. There are three legally relevant degrees of intoxication.
    • First, there is mild drunkenness, which induces relaxation of both inhibitions and socially acceptable behaviour. This has never been accepted as a factor in determining whether the accused possessed the requisite mens rea and the trial judge is not required to give any instruction on mild intoxication.
    • Second, there is advanced intoxication, i.e. intoxication to the point where the accused lacks the specific intent, to the extent of an impairment of the accused's foresight of the consequences of his or her act sufficient to raise a reasonable doubt about the requisite mens rea. A defence based on this level of intoxication applies only to specific intent offences and the extent of intoxication required to advance it successfully may vary, depending on the type of offence involved.
    • Third, there is extreme intoxication akin to automatism, which negates voluntariness and thus is a complete defence to criminal responsibility, but such a defence would be extremely rare and, by operation of s. 33.1 of the Criminal Code, limited to non violent types of offences. [40 44]

    Note that intoxication is not a "true defence" so to speak. Rather, its successful assertion will prevent Crown from proving the requisite actus reus (voluntariness) and/or mens rea (guilty state of mind) beyond a reasonable doubt.

    6.7 Mental Disorder

    An Accused who is deemed NCR (“Not Criminally Responsible on Account of Mental Disorder”) is neither found guilty nor acquitted. Instead of being criminally sanctioned, the offender must receive mental health treatment in a secure facility and can only be released based on a process set out in the Criminal Code, which is related to their level of risk.

    Section 16 of the Criminal Code

    Defence of mental disorder

    16(1) No person is criminally responsible for an act committed or an omission made while suffering from a mental disorder that rendered the person incapable of appreciating the nature and quality of the act or omission or of knowing that it was wrong.

    Presumption

    (2) Every person is presumed not to suffer from a mental disorder so as to be exempt from criminal responsibility by virtue of subsection (1), until the contrary is proved on the balance of probabilities.

    Burden of proof

    (3) The burden of proof that an accused was suffering from a mental disorder so as to be exempt from criminal responsibility is on the party that raises the issue.

    Mental disorder is not a true “defence”. The result of a successful mental disorder claim is a verdict of NCR.

    Disease of the Mind

    Section 2 of the Criminal Code defines “mental disorder” as a “disease of the mind”, preserving the common law definition that developed through case law.

    In Regina v. Simpson, 1977 CanLII 1142 (ON CA), the Ontario Court of Appeal found that it is for the judge to determine whether something is a disease of the mind or not. The key question for the judge is that while the existence of a disease of the mind is a necessary condition, it must operate to an extent that it renders the Accused either (a) incapable of appreciating the nature and quality of the act/omission, or (b) knowing that it is wrong.

    In Cooper v. R., 1979 CanLII 63 (SCC), Justice Dickson (as he then was) held that:

    the disease must be of such intensity as to render accused incapable of appreciating nature/quality of act or knowing it is wrong.”

    Appreciating the nature and quality of the act or omission

    In Cooper v. R., Justice Dickson:

    Was the accused person at the very time of the offence by reason of disease of the mind unable to fully appreciate not only the nature and quality of the act but the natural consequences that would flow from it? Was the accused person deprived of the mental capacity to foresee and measure the consequences of the act” (emphasis added).

    In R. v. Abbey, 1982 CanLII 25 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada held that an Accused who does not “appreciate the penal consequences of his actions” does not, by reason of that finding, fail to appreciate the nature and quality of the act under section 16 of the Criminal Code.

    Knowing that the Act is Wrong

    In R. v. Oommen, 1994 CanLII 101 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada revisited the interpretation of the word “wrong” in section 16 of the Criminal Code and overrode the decision in R. v. Chaulk. Justice McLachlin (as she then was) held that the test is

    whether the accused lacks the capacity to rationally decide whether the act is right or wrong and hence to make a rational choice about whether to do it or not.

    Discussion Activity: Mental Disorder Practice Problem

    Answer

    The accused does not appear to meet the NCR standard under s. 16 of the Criminal Code, which requires proof of three elements:

    • the accused was suffering from a mental disorder;
    • the mental disorder rendered the accused incapable of appreciating the nature and quality of the act or omission; or
    • incapable of knowing that the act was wrong.

    Section 2 of the Criminal Code defines “mental disorder” as a “disease of the mind.” In Regina v. Simpson, 1977 CanLII 1142 (ON CA), the Ontario Court of Appeal held that it is for the trial judge to determine whether a condition constitutes a disease of the mind. In Cooper v. R., 1979 CanLII 63 (SCC), the Supreme Court held that the disease must be of such intensity as to render the accused incapable of appreciating the nature and quality of the act, or of knowing that it was wrong.

    In this factual scenario, the accused was diagnosed by two psychiatrists as suffering from a mental disorder at the time of the homicide. Even so, it remains for the judge to determine whether the disorder was sufficiently severe to deprive the accused of the required capacity. Here, the court accepted the psychiatrists’ evidence as credible.

    With respect to “appreciating the nature and quality of the act,” the Court in Cooper explained that the real question is whether, at the very time of the offence, the accused was unable to fully appreciate not only the physical nature of the act, but also its natural consequences. In other words, was the accused deprived of the mental capacity to foresee and measure the consequences of the act?

    The Court also noted that s. 16 uses the word “appreciating,” rather than the narrower common law concept of merely “knowing” the nature and quality of the act. This makes NCR broader in scope than the former insanity defence.

    As for the requirement of knowing that the act was wrong, the Supreme Court in R. v. Oommen, 1994 CanLII 101 (SCC), revisited the meaning of “wrong” in s. 16 and held that the relevant inquiry is whether the accused lacked the capacity to rationally decide whether the act was right or wrong, and therefore to make a rational choice about whether to do it.

    On facts, the accused suffered from religious delusions and believed that “God” had commanded him to kill his daughter, failing which his entire family would be killed. However, this does not necessarily show that he lost the capacity to appreciate the nature and quality of his actions. He appears to have understood that killing his daughter would cause her death and that legal consequences would follow. Rather, he made a choice between what he perceived to be two evils: killing his daughter or risking the death of his entire family. He chose to preserve the rest of the family at the expense of his daughter’s life.

    The accused also appears to have understood that both options were morally wrong. After fatally stabbing his daughter, he went to church to pray, which strongly suggests an awareness that he had committed a wrongful act. The fact that he nevertheless proceeded indicates that he viewed the killing as the lesser wrong, not that he lacked the capacity to distinguish right from wrong.

    In my view, the accused’s imagined command from God functioned more like an abetting influence than evidence that his disease of the mind deprived him of the capacity required under s. 16. Accordingly, the accused should be convicted of murder rather than found NCR.

    Unit 7: Sentencing

    Within the range of possible sentences, the sentencing judge (who is the same as the trial judge) must fix a fit sentence that is proportionate to the gravity of the offence and the blameworthiness of the offender. The judge is guided by the general principles of sentencing found in Part XXIII of the Code and in the case law.

    For most offences, sentencing judges exercise their discretion within this range and are generally afforded deference by appeals courts, unless there is “an error in principle, failure to consider a relevant factor, or an overemphasis of the appropriate factors” such that the sentence is “demonstrably unfit” (R. v. M. (C.A.), 1996 CanLII 230 (SCC)).

    Sometimes the defence and the Crown make a joint submission as to the appropriate sentence, usually as part of a plea negotiation. The judge is not obliged to accept this recommendation and may choose to award a more or less severe sentence.

    7.2 Purpose and Objectives

    ss. 718 to 718.2 of the Criminal Code

    Purpose

    718 The fundamental purpose of sentencing is to protect society and to contribute, along with crime prevention initiatives, to respect for the law and the maintenance of a just, peaceful and safe society by imposing just sanctions that have one or more of the following objectives:

    • to denounce unlawful conduct and the harm done to victims or to the community that is caused by unlawful conduct;
    • to deter the offender and other persons from committing offences;
    • to separate offenders from society, where necessary;
    • to assist in rehabilitating offenders;
    • to provide reparations for harm done to victims or to the community; and
    • to promote a sense of responsibility in offenders, and acknowledgment of the harm done to victims or to the community.

    Fundamental principle

    718.1 A sentence must be proportionate to the gravity of the offence and the degree of responsibility of the offender.

    Other sentencing principles

    718.2 A court that imposes a sentence shall also take into consideration the following principles:

    (a) a sentence should be increased or reduced to account for any relevant aggravating or mitigating circumstances relating to the offence or the offender, and, without limiting the generality of the foregoing,

    • (i) evidence that the offence was motivated by bias, prejudice or hate based on race, national or ethnic origin, language, colour, religion, sex, age, mental or physical disability, sexual orientation, or gender identity or expression, or on any other similar factor,
    • (ii) evidence that the offender, in committing the offence, abused the offender’s spouse or common-law partner,
    • (ii.1) evidence that the offender, in committing the offence, abused a person under the age of eighteen years,
    • (iii) evidence that the offender, in committing the offence, abused a position of trust or authority in relation to the victim,
    • (iii.1) evidence that the offence had a significant impact on the victim, considering their age and other personal circumstances, including their health and financial situation,
    • (iv) evidence that the offence was committed for the benefit of, at the direction of or in association with a criminal organization,
    • (v) evidence that the offence was a terrorism offence, or
    • (vi) evidence that the offence was committed while the offender was subject to a conditional sentence order made under section 742.1 or released on parole, statutory release or unescorted temporary absence under the Corrections and Conditional Release Act shall be deemed to be aggravating circumstances;

    (b) a sentence should be similar to sentences imposed on similar offenders for similar offences committed in similar circumstances;

    (c) where consecutive sentences are imposed, the combined sentence should not be unduly long or harsh;

    Section 718 states that “[t]he fundamental purpose of sentencing is to protect society and to contribute, along with crime prevention initiatives, to respect for the law and the maintenance of a just, peaceful and safe society by imposing just sanctions that have one or more of the following objectives ...”

    7.3 Constitutional Considerations

    Section 12 of the Charter provides that

    everyone has the right not to be subjected to any cruel and unusual treatment or punishment.

    Most notably, this provision has been used, successfully in some cases, to challenge the imposition of mandatory minimum sentences of imprisonment (“MMPs”) for some offences. MMPs are the minimum sentence that a judge must impose for a given offence.

    Many of these MMPs were “struck down” by the courts over the first few years. The following cases of R. v. Nur, 2015 SCC 15 and R. v. Lloyd, 2016 SCC 13 are two of these decisions.

    At R. v. Nur, paragraph 46:

    [46] To recap, a challenge to a mandatory minimum sentencing provision on the ground it constitutes cruel and unusual punishment under s. 12 of the Charter involves two steps. First, the court must determine what constitutes a proportionate sentence for the offence having regard to the objectives and principles of sentencing in the Criminal Code. Then, the court must ask whether the mandatory minimum requires the judge to impose a sentence that is grossly disproportionate to the fit and proportionate sentence. If the answer is yes, the mandatory minimum provision is inconsistent with s. 12 and will fall unless justified under s. 1 of the Charter.

    7.4 Sentencing Options

    Not all types of sentence can be imposed for every offence or in every circumstance.

    • Absolute discharge: When a person is found guilty or pleads guilty but the judge decides it is not necessary to convict them. The person then has no criminal record. See Criminal Code section 730.
    • Conditional discharge: When a person is found guilty or pleads guilty but the judge decides it is not necessary to convict them if they obey the rules (conditions) of a probation order. If they obey all the rules, at the end of their probation they will not have a criminal record. See Criminal Code section 730. Note that a conditional discharge is very different from a conditional sentence.
    • Conditional sentence: A jail sentence served in the community. The offender does not live in jail but is supervised by BC Community Corrections. They must follow certain rules (conditions).
    • Suspended sentence and probation: With a suspended sentence, a judge convicts the offender, but suspends sentencing and instead releases the offender on specific conditions for a period of time, as set out in a probation order. This often includes a condition that the offender must report to a probation officer.
    • Fine: If the offender is given a fine, they will be given time to pay it. If it is not paid within that time, they could end up in custody or there could be other impacts such as being unable to renew their driver’s licence.
    • Custodial sentence: This is where the offender serves a period of time in jail, either a provincial correctional centre or a federal penitentiary.
    • Intermittent custodial sentence: For sentences of 90 days or less, a judge may order an intermittent custodial sentence. This means the offender does not serve all the days of the sentence at the same time. For example, they may serve the sentence on weekends.
    • Victim fine surcharge: The offender may be required to pay a surcharge or fee, called the victim surcharge, that goes toward helping victims of crime. This surcharge will be added by the court registry unless the judge says the offender does not have to pay it (exempts them from paying it) or reduces the amount. The judge may order that the offender pay no victim surcharge or that a reduced amount must be paid if the full amount would cause undue hardship because of financial circumstances. The judge may also decrease, increase or order no payment based on the circumstances of the offence and the offender's responsibility for it.

    In R. v. Proulx, 2000 SCC 5, the Supreme Court of Canada explains the significance of the conditional sentence of imprisonment and sets out the test for when it is an available and appropriate sentence. Note that conditional sentences were no longer available for many drug trafficking offences since the passing of the Safe Streets and Community Act discussed in the previous topic, however these prohibitions on CSOs were later overturned as unconstitutional.

    R. v. Proulx, headnote

    A conditional sentence should be distinguished from probationary measures. Probation is primarily a rehabilitative sentencing tool. By contrast, Parliament intended conditional sentences to include both punitive and rehabilitative aspects. Therefore, conditional sentences should generally include punitive conditions that are restrictive of the offender's liberty. Conditions such as house arrest should be the norm, not the exception.

    No offences are excluded from the conditional sentencing regime except those with a minimum term of imprisonment, nor should there be presumptions in favour of or against a conditional sentence for specific offences.

    Section 742.1 of the Code lists four criteria that a court must consider before deciding to impose a conditional sentence:

    • (1) the offender must be convicted of an offence that is not punishable by a minimum term of imprisonment;
    • (2) the court must impose a term of imprisonment of less than two years;
    • (3) the safety of the community would not be endangered by the offender serving the sentence in the community; and
    • (4) a conditional sentence would be consistent with the fundamental purpose and principles of sentencing set out in ss. 718 to 718.2.

    7.5 Aboriginal Offenders

    In R. v. Gladue, 1999 CanLII 679 (SCC), the Supreme Court of Canada recognized the problem of disproportionate incarceration of Aboriginal offenders and established the approach that should be taken in sentencing such offenders, citing section 718.2(e) of the Criminal Code.

    Section 718.2(e) of the Criminal Code states that

    all available sanctions other than imprisonment that are reasonable in the circumstances should be considered for all offenders, with particular attention to the circumstances of aboriginal offenders.

    7.6 Victims

    Victim Impact Statements

    The main role of victims during sentencing is to provide information to the sentencing judge about the impact of the offence on them through a victim impact statement. This document may be submitted in written form or read aloud during the sentencing hearing.

    ss 722 to 722.2 of the Criminal Code

    Victim impact statement

    722(1) When determining the sentence to be imposed on an offender or determining whether the offender should be discharged under section 730 in respect of any offence, the court shall consider any statement of a victim prepared in accordance with this section and filed with the court describing the physical or emotional harm, property damage or economic loss suffered by the victim as the result of the commission of the offence and the impact of the offence on the victim.

    Community impact statement

    722.2(1) When determining the sentence to be imposed on an offender or determining whether the offender should be discharged under section 730 in respect of any offence, the court shall consider any statement made by an individual on a community’s behalf that was prepared in accordance with this section and filed with the court describing the harm or loss suffered by the community as the result of the commission of the offence and the impact of the offence on the community.
