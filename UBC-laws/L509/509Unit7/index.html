    Standard of Review.

    7.0 Overview

    The landmark case in the choice and application of the standard of review in Canada is the 2019 decision of Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) v. Vavilov, 2019 SCC 65.

    A very quick and brief reminder on the two current standards of reviewing an administrative decision:

    • Reasonableness: is the more deferential standard that a court can give to an administrative decision-maker based on expertise of the decision-maker. A decision must be shown to be “unreasonable” in the sense that no reasons are given in analysis to reasonably support the conclusion. Following the Vavilov decision it is clearly the presumptive (i.e. “default”) standard of reviewing administrative decisions, including ones that involve jurisdictional questions of a administrative decision-maker exceeding their statutory authority.
    • Correctness: is the less deferential standard that a court can give to an administrative decision-maker. The court will generally give no deference at all to the decision-maker, will base its ruling on application of law, and can substitute its own opinion for the administrative decision-maker. Correctness review applies in certain statutorily directed situations and other exceptional situations outlined in Vavilov.

    7.1 A Short History of Standard of Review

    Readings

    • Textbook, pp 307-314

    Module Content

    In 1979, the SCC in C.U.P.E. v. N.B. Liquor Corporation, 1979 CanLII 23 (SCC) charted a new course that encouraged courts to defer to administrative decision-makers who provided reasonable answers to legal questions within their mandates.

    Generally, the basic starting point for the standard of review of an administrative process is that it does NOT involve a total reassessment of the merits of a decision. There are some exceptions to reasonableness review, now outlined in Vavilov, that will attract correctness review.

    As we will see in the 2019 Vavilvov decision, the SCC relegated jurisdictional questions (other than overlapping jurisdiction) to reasonableness review. However, the “language of jurisdiction” lives on and understanding the concept of jurisdiction is still fundamentally important to provide context for a deeper study into applicable standards of review in Canadian administrative law.

    The "Blockbuster" case: CUPE

    CUPE dealt with many issues, including statutory interpretation and the extent of deference to be shown to administrative tribunals in areas of their expertise.

    The case is summarized in text. Read the case as a good historical explanation of theory and principle and less for its current precedential value.

    Textbook Summary

    In administrative law doctrine, the standard of review inquiry proceeds in two steps. First, should the court defer? Second, if the answer is yes, how does the court defer.

    The jurisprudence has been volatile, and the Supreme Court of Canada has hit the “reset” button several times. It did so in 1979 (CUPE), 1998 (Pushpanathan v. Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration), 1998 CanLII 778 (SCC)), and again in 2008 (Dunsmuir v. New Brunswick, 2008 SCC 9) before the latest revision in Vavilov (2019).

    Judicial review is ordinarily available for breaches of procedural fairness, errors of law, abuse of discretion, or factual findings made in the absence of evidence.

    So-called privative clauses were originally intended to prevent courts from interfering with substantive outcomes of administrative action through the doctrines of error of law or absence of evidence for findings of fact. Privative clauses vary in wording but usually include a grant of exclusive jurisdiction over the subject matter, a declaration of finality with respect to the outcome, and a prohibition on any court proceedings to set the outcome aside.

    On the one hand, the common law presumes that citizens retain access to the ordinary courts in order to ensure that creatures of statute do not exceed or abuse the power granted to them. The Supreme Court of Canada even elevated judicial review to a constitutionally protected principle under s 96 of the Constitution Act, 1867.

    On the other hand, the doctrine of parliamentary supremacy dictates that the legislator enacts the law, and the court must interpret and apply the law in accordance with the legislator’s intent.

    the House of Lords’ Anisminic Ltd v Foreign Compensation Commission, [1969] 2 AC 147 (HL) case: decision-makers’ jurisdiction (authority to act) is demarcated by statute.

    Judges faced with a privative clause assigned themselves the task of determining whether the issue fell “within jurisdiction” and was thereby insulated from judicial review, OR was a “jurisdictional question” that determined the outer boundary of the decision-maker’s authority.

    In a case of the latter, a court was entitled to review the decision as if no privative clause existed.

    The most enduring effect of this jurisprudence was the persistence of the concept of a “jurisdictional question,” long after standard of review doctrine evolved to a point where a “jurisdictional question” was no longer required to justify judicial review in the face of a privative clause.

    CUPE V NEW BRUNSWICK LIQUOR CORPORATION

    The shorthand description of CUPE’s outcome is that a jurisdictional question is assessed according to a standard of “correctness,” while questions within jurisdiction are evaluated against a standard of “patent unreasonableness.”

    The Court’s judgment in CUPE did not make a clean break with earlier jurisprudence that invoked jurisdictional error to circumvent privative clauses. It conveyed a spirit of curial deference, a recognition that administrative decision-makers are not merely “inferior tribunals” but specialized bodies that possess a legislative mandate to apply their expertise and experience to matters that they may be better suited to address than an “ordinary court.”

    Consequently, a court should only intervene (by labelling as jurisdictional error) where an interpretation of the provision is “so patently unreasonable that its construction cannot be rationally supported by the relevant legislation.”

    A reading of CUPE reveals three sources of the Supreme Court of Canada’s doctrinal change.

    • First, ... according to Dickson J, courts should recognize that these specialized decision-makers bear primary responsibility for implementing their statutory mandate and may bring valuable expertise and experience to the task that a generalist judge lacks.
    • Second, no single interpretation could lay claim to being “correct.” Instead, there were several plausible interpretations.
    • Finally, Dickson J acknowledges the failure of prior judicial efforts to construct a coherent, principled means of distinguishing reviewable questions from those insulated by a privative clause.

    7.2 Concept of Curial Deference

    Module Content

    Curial Deference

    • B.C. (Health sciences Assn. of) v. British Columbia (Industrial Relations Council), 1992 CanLII 373 (BC CA) at paras 33-34
      • The term “Curial deference” is a way of expressing the notion that when the Legislature sets up a specialized tribunal, invests it with broad powers and incorporates a privative clause into the enabling statute, it is telling the courts that it intends the tribunal to have the right, because it understands the subject-matter better than do judges, to make decisions which the judges might think to be wrong decisions.
      • To put it another way, where, by the terms of its legislation, the Legislature requires curial deference, the courts are bound, where no constitutional question arises, to obey the Legislature and not subvert its intention.
    • Vavilov, at para 286
      • Curial deference, after all, is the hallmark of reasonableness review, setting it apart from the substitution of opinion permitted under the correctness standard.

    You should note that there are many past SCC decisions that recognize that privative clauses were relevant indicators of legislative intent and attracted increase judicial deference to an administrative decision-maker. This is something that may have changed with the SCC’s “recalibration” of standard of review in Vavilov.

    In the Vavilov framework, privative clauses by themselves do not seem particularly relevant in choosing a standard of review. Absent a clear statutory avenue of appeal or other statutory language, or the presence of one of the exceptions outlined in Vavilov, a presumption of reasonableness review will now apply whether or not there is a privative clause in an administrative decision-maker’s home statute.

    7.3 Pragmatic and Functional Analysis

    Readings

    • Textbook, pp 314-319

    Module Content

    The SCC’s historical definition of P&FA was reformulated in Pushpanathan. The question to be answered became a contextual one:

    “Did the legislator intend this question to attract judicial deference?”

    The factors to be analyzed included privative clause, tribunal expertise, act’s purpose and the nature of the problem in issue. In terms of applying the PF&A approach, Pushpanathan is now sidelined by the landmark Vavilov decision in 2019.

    When reading the text readings, consider the 4 elements from Pushpanathan test (Privative Clause, Expertise, General Statutory Purpose & Specific Purpose of. Provision, & Nature of the Problem) and how they have changed in subsequent SCC decisions.

    As you will see in the next topics, the effects of the landmark SCC decisions in Dunsmuir and Vavilov altered the consideration of these contextual factors.

    Vavilov in particular criticized the PF&A's contextual analysis as "unwieldly" offering "limited practical guidance for courts attempting to assess an administrative decision maker’s relative expertise. " (Vavilov, para 27).

    Textbook Summary

    FROM CUPE TO PUSHPANATHAN

    In terms of the rule-of-law tension between fidelity to legislative intent and the common law presumption of access to the ordinary courts, the Court’s expansive interpretation of s 96 of the Constitution Act, 1867 ensures that the latter trumps the former.

    Section 96 grants jurisdiction over the appointment of superior court judges to the federal government but has been interpreted to protect “essential” judicial functions associated with superior court judges.

    Discussion 7.3 Historical Standard of Review 1

    Question

    Summarize how the Standard of Review for questions within an ADM's jurisdiction changed over time from the CUPE, Southam and Pushpanathan decisions.

    Samantha's Comment

    The standard of review refers to the level of deference that a reviewing court gives to an administrative decision maker's (ADM) decision. The standard of review can have a significant impact on the outcome of a judicial review.

    In the CUPE decision, the Supreme Court of Canada (SCC) established the "pragmatic and functional approach," which requires the court to consider several factors, including the expertise of the ADM and the nature of the question being decided, in determining the appropriate standard of review. This approach gives a high level of deference to ADMs, as long as their decisions are within their jurisdiction and are reasonable.

    In the Southam decision, the SCC clarified that the standard of review analysis should be conducted on a question-by-question basis, rather than on the decision as a whole. This means that different questions within a decision may be subject to different standards of review.

    In Pushpanathan, the SCC further refined the standard of review analysis by establishing two categories of questions:

    • (1) "jurisdictional" questions, which determine whether the ADM has the authority to make a particular decision, and
    • (2) "non-jurisdictional" questions, which concern the substance of the decision.

    The SCC held that jurisdictional questions should be reviewed on a standard of correctness, while non-jurisdictional questions should be reviewed on a standard of reasonableness.

    Overall, these decisions have resulted in a more nuanced and contextual approach to the standard of review, which seeks to balance the need for deference to ADMs with the need for effective judicial oversight.
